## 3. Core concepts

### 3.1 Incoterms — the four groups
Incoterms define where along the transport chain the seller's responsibility ends and the buyer's begins. Coverage concerns four things: **freight, insurance, duties/tariffs/taxes**.

- **E group (e.g., EXW — Ex Works)** — Seller's responsibility ends at their own premises (factory). Buyer picks up at the seller's location and assumes freight, insurance, taxes, duties, and everything else.
- **F group (e.g., FCA, FAS, FOB)** — Seller is responsible up to the port of departure in the seller's country (example: Mexico → Europe, seller responsible up to the port of Veracruz on the Gulf Coast). Sub-variants distinguish whether the point of transfer is on the wharf, alongside the ship, at the ship's rail, or already inside the vessel — the instructor explicitly said he will **not** test sub-variants within a group.
- **C group (e.g., CFR, CIF, CPT, CIP)** — Seller is responsible up to the port of arrival (example: Rotterdam, Netherlands for interior-Europe shipments). The buyer then takes the goods from the port of arrival to the final destination.
- **D group (e.g., DAP, DPU, DDP)** — Seller is responsible all the way to the final point of delivery in the buyer's interior (example: Freiburg im Breisgau, Germany). DDP extends this to include customs clearance.

## 4. Definitions / terminology

|------|------------|
| Incoterms | Standardised international commercial terms defining allocation of cost, risk, insurance, freight, and duties between seller and buyer. |
| EXW (Ex Works) | E-group term; seller delivers at own premises, buyer bears all onward costs and risks. |
| FOB / FCA / FAS | F-group terms; seller delivers to a carrier or port of departure, buyer takes over from there. |

### Q1: Compare and contrast the explanations of FDI: Internalization theory, Strategic Behavior theory of Knickerbocker and the Eclectic Paradigm.
*Book: p. 231 ->*

Internalization theory explains foreign direct investment as a response to market imperfections, arguing that firms choose FDI over licensing when they cannot adequately protect valuable know-how, need tight control over operations and strategy, or rely on capabilities such as management or organizational culture that cannot be easily transferred through contracts, so FDI becomes the most efficient way to exploit these advantages. In contrast, Knickerbocker's strategic behavior theory focuses on competition in oligopolistic industries, where firms undertake FDI not primarily for efficiency but to match rivals' moves and avoid competitive disadvantage, leading to imitative investment patterns and multipoint competition across markets. The eclectic paradigm developed by Dunning combines these perspectives and adds location-specific advantages, arguing that FDI occurs when three conditions are met: firms possess ownership advantages such as technology or brand, it is beneficial to internalize these rather than license them, and there are location advantages such as natural resources, low-cost skilled labor, or knowledge clusters that make it attractive to produce in a particular place. The paradigm thus provides a more comprehensive explanation by integrating firm-specific, strategic, and location-based factors.

### Q2: What are the principal costs and benefits of FDI for both the home and host country?
*Book: p. 237 ->*

The principal benefits of FDI for a **host country** include resource-transfer effects, where foreign firms bring capital, technology, and managerial know-how that increase productivity and economic growth; employment effects through both direct job creation and indirect jobs in local supply chains; improvements in the balance of payments when FDI substitutes for imports or generates exports; and increased competition, which can lower prices, improve efficiency, and stimulate innovation. However, host countries also face costs such as the risk that powerful multinational firms may reduce competition or dominate local markets, negative balance-of-payments effects due to profit repatriation or import of inputs, and concerns over loss of national sovereignty if key economic decisions are controlled by foreign entities. For the **home country**, benefits include improved balance of payments from repatriated profits and exports to foreign subsidiaries, positive employment effects when domestic firms supply inputs to their foreign operations, and reverse knowledge transfer as firms learn from foreign markets and bring back new capabilities. The costs for the home country include an initial capital outflow that may worsen the balance of payments, potential long-term trade deficits if production shifts abroad or replaces exports, and job losses when firms relocate production to lower-cost countries, especially where FDI substitutes for domestic production.

### Q3: What are the principal weaknesses of Licensing vs. FDI?
*Book: p. 231 ->*

The principal weaknesses of licensing compared to foreign direct investment are that licensing can lead to a loss of valuable know-how, since the licensee may become a future competitor after learning the technology; it provides limited control over production, marketing, and strategy, which can reduce profitability and risk damaging the firm's brand; and it is often ineffective when a firm's competitive advantage is based on complex capabilities such as management, organizational culture, or processes that cannot be easily transferred through contracts, meaning the licensee may not operate as efficiently or fully exploit the market potential. In such situations FDI is preferred because it allows the firm to retain control and better utilize its strengths.

### Q4: Firms engaged in international trade must do business with people they cannot trust and with whom it may be difficult to track down if they default on an obligation. How can each party protect themselves in these situations and how would such a defense work?
*Book: p. 474 -> (letter of credit)*

In international trade where parties may not trust each other, both sides can protect themselves using financial instruments such as a **letter of credit**, which shifts the risk to a trusted third party, typically a bank. In a letter-of-credit arrangement, the importer's bank guarantees payment to the exporter as long as the exporter provides the required shipping and documentation proving the goods have been sent according to the agreed conditions. This protects the exporter because payment is secured by the bank rather than the buyer, and it protects the importer because payment is only made after the exporter fulfills the contract terms. The process involves the importer requesting the letter of credit from their bank, the bank issuing it to the exporter's bank, and payment being made once documents are verified. In addition to letters of credit, other safeguards can include advance payments, documentary collections, or using intermediaries, but the key idea is that risk is reduced by relying on trusted financial institutions and formal documentation rather than direct trust between the trading parties.

### Q5: What are the differences among the main groups of Incoterms?
*From class notes (not found in book)*

The main groups of Incoterms differ in how responsibilities, costs, and risks are divided between the exporter and the importer during transportation. In the **E group**, such as EXW, the seller has minimal responsibility and the buyer takes on almost all costs and risks from the seller's premises. In the **F group**, including FCA, FAS, and FOB, the seller delivers the goods to a carrier chosen by the buyer, after which the risk transfers to the buyer. In the **C group**, such as CFR, CIF, CPT, and CIP, the seller pays for transportation to the destination but the risk transfers to the buyer once the goods are handed over to the carrier, meaning cost and risk are split. In the **D group**, including DAP, DPU, and DDP, the seller bears most of the responsibility, cost, and risk by delivering the goods to the buyer's location, often including customs clearance in the most extreme case, DDP. Overall, the key difference between the groups is how far along the transport chain the seller is responsible and at what point risk transfers to the buyer.

### Q6: To the best of your knowledge, what is an example of a joint venture that has worked well over the long run and what is an example of a joint venture that has not worked well and hence was dissolved?
*Theory about joint ventures: Book: p. 442 ->*
*Case: Book: p. 626 ->*

A successful long-term joint venture is the **General Motors and SAIC Motor** partnership in China, where both firms combined complementary strengths: GM provided technology and global expertise while SAIC contributed local market knowledge and government connections, allowing them to adapt products to local demand and achieve strong growth and profitability. In contrast, a well-known failed joint venture is the **Daimler-Benz and Chrysler Corporation** merger, which was effectively a joint-venture-like integration that collapsed due to cultural differences, conflicting management styles, and disagreements over strategy, leading to poor performance and eventual separation. This illustrates how misaligned goals, lack of control clarity, and cultural clashes can cause joint ventures to fail despite initial strategic logic.

### Q7: What is your opinion regarding the use of tariffs in international trade? (Addressed in Chapter 7 of the book.)
*Definition: Book: p. 195*
*Import quotas: Book: p. 197*
*Export tariffs and bans: Book: p. 198*
*Protecting Jobs and Industries: Book: p. 201*
*Retaliation: Book: p. 202*

Tariffs can be justified in international trade because they protect domestic industries and jobs from foreign competition, generate government revenue, and can be used strategically to counter unfair trade practices or pressure other countries to open their markets. However, overall they tend to have more negative than positive effects because they raise prices for consumers, reduce competition, and lead to inefficient allocation of resources by encouraging production in locations where it is not cost efficient. They may also trigger retaliation from other countries, resulting in trade wars that harm all parties. So while tariffs can be useful in specific situations — such as protecting infant industries or responding to unfair subsidies — in general they reduce overall economic welfare and are considered harmful to global trade efficiency.
