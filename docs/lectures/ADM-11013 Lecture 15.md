## 1. Overview

This lecture shifts focus from the balance of payments (macro) to the **firm** (micro), specifically how an export manager should determine the cost — and therefore the minimum acceptable price — of an exported product. Two costing methods are contrasted (**direct / variable costing** vs. **absorbent / full costing**), and the link to **dumping** accusations is made explicit. The rest of the session is a worked exercise (drawn from Bancomext — Mexico's Bank of Foreign Trade — executive-education material) in which students must separate useful data from decoy data and compute the break-even export price.

## 3. Core concepts

### 3.1 Thinking like an export manager

Before calculating anything, an export manager — especially in a small or family-owned firm — must build a mental **checklist** of everything required to move a product abroad. In large multinationals much of this is pre-planned, but understanding the logic from scratch is essential.

A firm may deliberately **sell abroad cheaper than in the home market** — the "icing on the cake" idea: export sales are incremental, and the firm is happy to clear excess inventory at a discount. But **the home market remains what really matters** for most firms; exports should not subsidise losses at home.

### 3.2 Two methods of cost determination

Two standard methods are used to determine the cost (and hence the floor price) of an exported good:

1. **Direct costing (a.k.a. variable costing)**
  - Assigns **only variable costs** to the product.
  - Fixed costs are treated as period costs, recovered from total contribution — not attributed per unit exported.
  - Yields a **lower** per-unit floor price → firm can charge less abroad.
2. **Absorbent costing (a.k.a. absorption / full costing)**
  - The product **absorbs both fixed and variable costs**.
  - Each unit carries a share of fixed manufacturing, fixed labor (in Mexico), and fixed operating costs.
  - Yields a **higher** per-unit floor price.

**Variable cost**: per-unit cost that scales with output (materials, variable operating cost).
**Fixed cost**: does not change with output within the relevant range — e.g., property tax, fixed plant overhead, labor (in Mexico). Fixed cost is fixed **no matter whether you produce 1,000,000 units, 500,000, or 1,000**.

### 3.3 Labor: fixed or variable?

- **Mexico / most European countries**: labor is **fixed**. Workers have contracts that must be respected; termination requires legally-mandated liquidation payments (formula based on months of salary).
- **United States**: labor behaves like a **variable** cost because at-will employment allows firms to dismiss workers quickly without mandated severance.
- Consequence: the US labor market is more flexible → more hiring churn but less employee protection.

### 3.4 Fixed rate of application

When absorbent costing is used, fixed costs must be allocated per unit using **normal production capacity** (not actual output):

$$\text{Fixed rate of application} = \frac{\text{Total fixed cost}}{\text{Normal capacity}}$$

From the exercise data:

- **Fixed manufacturing rate**: $25{,}000{,}000 \div 50{,}000 \text{ units} = 500/\text{unit}$
- **Fixed labor rate**: $20{,}000{,}000 \div 50{,}000 \text{ hours} = 400/\text{hour}$

Critically: if actual production is below normal capacity, the **total fixed cost does not fall** — only the per-unit absorbed portion changes (or, more precisely, under-absorption occurs). This is the trap the exercise sets.

### 3.5 Dumping

**Dumping** = selling a product in a foreign market **below the (home-market or cost-based) market price**, typically as a **short-term** predatory strategy: accept losses now to destroy the competition, then raise prices once rivals have left the market. Governments respond with **anti-dumping duties** (tariffs applied on suspect products).

Which costing method exposes a firm to dumping accusations?

- **Direct cost**: only variable costs are covered. The firm may price abroad well below its absorbent (full) cost in the home market → foreign authorities see a below-cost/below-home-market price → accusation of dumping.
- **Absorbent cost**: every exported unit carries its share of fixed costs → export price is closer to (or equal to) home price → much safer.

**Answer: direct cost method is the one most susceptible to dumping accusations.**

Real-world Mexican examples mentioned: **CEMEX** (Cementos Mexicanos) — the big cement producer, repeatedly accused of dumping cement abroad at prices below domestic Mexican ones; and **Vasos** / glass producers (Vitro group likely — transcription uncertain). Many companies worldwide face such accusations; Mexican firms are not uniquely targeted.

### 3.6 Decoy information (didactic point)

The exercise deliberately contains **false-friend data** ("a little bit of straw") — information meant to deceive. The real world dumps data on a manager in chaotic form; sorting useful from useless is part of the skill.

- The **per-hour** labor rate ($400/hour) and the per-unit **fixed rate of application** ($500/unit) are examples of potentially decoy figures: they look like unit costs that scale with production, but **fixed totals don't move**.

## 4. Definitions / terminology


| Term                                       | Definition                                                                                                                                                                  |
| ------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Direct cost (variable costing)             | Costing method that assigns only variable costs to a product; fixed costs are treated as period expenses.                                                                   |
| Absorbent cost (absorption / full costing) | Costing method under which a product absorbs **both** fixed and variable costs per unit.                                                                                    |
| Variable cost                              | Cost that scales with units produced (materials, variable operating costs, US-style labor).                                                                                 |
| Fixed cost                                 | Cost independent of output volume within the relevant range (property tax, plant overhead, Mexican labor, fixed operating cost).                                            |
| Fixed rate of application                  | Total fixed cost ÷ normal production capacity; allocates fixed costs per unit (or per labor hour).                                                                          |
| Normal production capacity                 | Standard expected output used as the denominator for absorbing fixed costs (50,000 units/month in the exercise).                                                            |
| Dumping                                    | Selling a product in a foreign market below market price (often below full cost) for short-term strategic reasons — typically to destroy competition before raising prices. |
| Anti-dumping measure                       | Government response (usually a tariff/duty) on imports deemed to be dumped.                                                                                                 |
| Marginal gain/loss                         | Incremental profit or loss from accepting an additional order, computed using only incremental (variable) costs vs. incremental revenue.                                    |
| Break-even price                           | Price at which no profit and no loss is made — just enough to clear the merchandise and cover costs.                                                                        |


## 5. Worked exercise — Super Brothers export decision

### 5.1 Data given (to be sorted: some is decoy)

- Cost categories listed: direct materials, labor, variable manufacturing, fixed manufacturing; sum = **total cost of production**.
- Variable operating cost: **$100 / unit**.
- Fixed operating cost: **$2,000,000 / month**.
- Fixed manufacturing cost: **$25,000,000**; normal capacity: **50,000 units/month** → fixed manufacturing rate = **$500/unit**.
- Labor (fixed in Mexico): normal capacity **50,000 hours/period**, total fixed labor cost **$20,000,000** → fixed labor rate = **$400/hour**.
- Total variable cost per unit used in the exercise: **$900/unit** (combined figure students work with).
- Current production / sales: **30,000 units/month** domestically at **$2,000/unit**.
- New offer: a foreign client wants to buy **10,000 additional units** at **$1,300/unit** (so total production would be 40,000 units).

### 5.2 Consolidated cost structure at 40,000 units produced


| Item                                | Amount          |
| ----------------------------------- | --------------- |
| Variable cost total (40,000 × $900) | **$36,000,000** |
| Fixed manufacturing                 | $25,000,000     |
| Fixed labor                         | $20,000,000     |
| Fixed operating                     | $2,000,000      |
| **Total fixed**                     | **$47,000,000** |
| **Total cost (fixed + variable)**   | **$83,000,000** |


Revenue currently realised on domestic market:
$$30{,}000 \times 2{,}000 = 60{,}000{,}000$$

### 5.3 Question A — Direct-cost method: accept or reject the $1,300 offer?

Under **direct costing**, only variable cost is relevant to the incremental decision.

- Variable cost per exported unit: **$900** (per notes' figure).
- Offered price: **$1,300**.
- Marginal gain per unit: $1{,}300 - 900 = 400$.
- Total marginal gain on the order: $10{,}000 \times 400 = \boxed{4{,}000{,}000}$.

**Answer: YES — accept.** The order produces a marginal **gain of $4,000,000** because every incremental unit sold above variable cost contributes to covering fixed costs (which are already being paid anyway) and then to profit.

### 5.4 Question B — Absorbent-cost method: minimum (break-even) export price?

Under **absorbent costing**, the firm must recover **all** costs (fixed + variable) from total sales. The minimum export price is the one at which total revenue = total cost → **no profit, no loss, just clear the warehouse**.

Step 1 — Total cost to cover at 40,000 units: **$83,000,000**.

Step 2 — Revenue already secured from domestic sales:
$$30{,}000 \times 2{,}000 = 60{,}000{,}000$$

Step 3 — Remaining amount that the 10,000 export units must generate to break even:
$$83{,}000{,}000 - 60{,}000{,}000 = 23{,}000{,}000$$

Step 4 — Minimum price per export unit:
$$\frac{23{,}000{,}000}{10{,}000 \text{ units}} = \boxed{2{,}300 / \text{unit}}$$

**Answer: the manufacturer would demand a minimum of $2,300/unit** using absorbent costing. Since the client only offers $1,300, the firm would **reject** under absorbent logic (it would book a loss of $(2{,}300 - 1{,}300) \times 10{,}000 = 10{,}000{,}000$ of unabsorbed cost on that order).

### 5.5 Question C — Which method is more exposed to dumping accusations?

**Direct cost.** Because the export price ($1,300) is below both the domestic price ($2,000) and the absorbent-cost break-even ($2,300), foreign governments will see a below-market, below-full-cost sale and may file dumping complaints.

### 5.6 Decoy data to recognise

- **Fixed rate of application** ($500/unit) and **fixed labor rate** ($400/hour) **look** like unit/hourly costs that should scale with production, but total fixed costs **do not change** with output. Using them as per-unit variable inputs is the trap.
- The hint from the lecturer: *"If we only produce 40,000 units, then the fixed cost is lower — **NO.** The fixed cost is fixed whether you produce one unit or 50,000 units."*
