# T12 — FDI Theories: Internalization, Knickerbocker, Dunning

**Appears in lectures:** L20, L21, L22
**Textbook:** Hill Ch. 8 (FDI theory); p. 231 onwards (edition varies — p. 231 in instructor's copy; verify your edition)
**Exam priority:** VERY HIGH — the **Silicon Valley / Eclectic Paradigm** question is explicitly teacher-flagged (must use the phrases "location-specific advantages" and "externalities"). The three theories were also covered in L20 and appear as L22 Control Question 1 (take-home practice, not itself an exam flag).

## 1. TL;DR
The three FDI theories are complementary explanations for **why firms serve foreign markets through FDI rather than exporting or licensing**. **Internalization theory** says FDI arises from market imperfections — firms "internalize" transactions when contracts (licensing) cannot adequately protect know-how, enforce tight control, or transfer tacit capabilities. **Knickerbocker's Strategic Behavior theory** explains FDI as imitative, herd-like moves inside oligopolies — rivals match each other's international investments to preserve competitive parity rather than to maximize efficiency. **Dunning's Eclectic Paradigm (OLI)** synthesizes the other two and adds the missing piece — **location**: FDI occurs where Ownership, Location, and Internalization advantages coincide.

## 2. Internalization theory

### Core idea
- FDI is a response to **market imperfections** (also called the **market-imperfections approach**).
- Firms **internalize** transactions — bring them inside the firm — when arm's-length markets (i.e., licensing contracts) fail to deliver adequate value.
- Ownership internalizes value that a contract cannot secure.
- Spelling matters: **internalization**, not "internationalization."

### When it predicts FDI
A firm chooses FDI over licensing when:
1. Valuable **know-how cannot be adequately protected** by a licensing contract (the licensee could become a competitor).
2. **Tight control** over production, marketing, and strategy is needed to maximize profitability.
3. Competitive advantage rests on **capabilities not amenable to licensing** — management practices, organizational culture, tacit/firm-specific skills.

### Link to licensing limits
The three weaknesses of licensing (see [T11](T11%20-%20FDI%20vs%20Licensing.md)) **ARE** the internalization argument — each weakness of a contract is simultaneously a reason to internalize via ownership.

## 3. Knickerbocker's Strategic Behavior theory

### Core idea
- FDI as **imitative / copycat behavior** inside **oligopolistic industries**.
- When one major rival invests abroad, competitors follow **to avoid competitive disadvantage**, not because FDI is objectively the most efficient mode.
- The driver is **strategic rivalry**, not efficiency.

### Implications
- **Imitative / herd-like FDI patterns** — waves of investment clustered around rivals' moves.
- **Multipoint competition** — rivals end up meeting each other in multiple country markets, which stabilizes the oligopoly through mutual deterrence.
- Classic example: **Boeing vs. Airbus** — Boeing builds in South Carolina; Airbus responds with a plant in Alabama. Boeing also has operations near Airbus' European footprint (Sheffield, UK). Also **Coca-Cola vs. Pepsi** as an oligopolistic pairing.

### Limitations
- Does **not explain first-mover FDI** — someone has to move first, and Knickerbocker is silent on why.
- Does **not apply** to non-oligopolistic industries (competitive markets with many small firms).
- Says nothing about **where** the FDI should go (no location logic).
- Note: the textbook discusses the theory; **Knickerbocker** is the classical attribution but Hill doesn't always name him explicitly.

## 4. Dunning's Eclectic Paradigm (OLI)

### Three conditions (must all hold)
FDI occurs when three advantages coincide:

- **O — Ownership advantages** (firm-specific): the firm has something rivals lack — proprietary **technology**, a strong **brand**, distinctive **organizational culture** or managerial capabilities.
- **L — Location advantages** (country-/place-specific): the foreign location offers **natural resources**, **low-cost skilled labor**, **knowledge clusters**, infrastructure, or market access that make producing *there* superior.
- **I — Internalization advantages**: it is more efficient to **exploit the O advantages through internal control** (FDI) than through contracts (licensing). This is Dunning reusing the internalization argument.

### Why it's "eclectic"
- It combines three strands into one framework:
  - the **internalization** argument (I),
  - firm-specific **ownership** factors (O), which overlap with strategic factors,
  - and the missing **location** dimension (L).
- It is the **most comprehensive** of the three frameworks.
- Correct term: **"Eclectic Paradigm"** (instructor sometimes mis-says "Eclectic Paradise" / "Paradox" — these are slips).

### Externalities / spillovers
- Within a geographic cluster, knowledge **"spills over"** between co-located firms — engineers move between firms, suppliers share practices, universities feed talent locally.
- The **textbook term is "externalities"** (sometimes "knowledge spillovers"). Use both phrases.
- Externalities are the **theoretical basis for why clustering produces location-specific advantages** — they make the *whole cluster* more productive than any single firm could be alone.

**Example — Silicon Valley (USA):**
- **Stanford and UC Berkeley** produced a dense supply of PhDs and technical talent.
- Firms **co-located** (Apple, Google, Oracle, HP, etc.) to draw from the same labor pool and benefit from knowledge externalities.
- Labor moved freely between neighboring firms, reinforcing the spillover.
- The cluster became **self-reinforcing** — talent attracts firms, firms attract more talent.

**Other cluster examples (course):**
- **Automobile industry** — Detroit historically (US); Baden-Württemberg / Bavaria (southern Germany).
- **Petroleum / energy** — Houston and Gulf Coast (US); also Mexico.
- **Banking** — Switzerland (Zurich, Geneva).
- **Distribution / logistics** — Netherlands (Rotterdam + highway network).
- **Agriculture / commodities** — Chicago, historically.
- **Technology in Mexico** — Guadalajara area.

## 5. Comparison table (STUDY THIS)

| Theory | Core driver | Best explains | Main limitation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Internalization** | Market failures / need for control over know-how | FDI in high-tech, complex or culture-dependent capabilities | Doesn't address **location** choice |
| **Knickerbocker (Strategic Behavior)** | Competitive rivalry in **oligopolies** | Imitative FDI between rivals (Boeing/Airbus) | Doesn't explain the **first mover** or FDI outside oligopolies; no location logic |
| **Eclectic Paradigm (OLI)** | Combined **O + L + I** conditions | General, unified FDI framework | Can be too broad / hard to falsify |

## 6. Key terminology

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Internalization** | Bringing a transaction inside the firm (ownership) rather than using an arm's-length contract (licensing); the value of ownership vs. contract. |
| **Market-imperfections approach** | Alternate name for internalization theory — FDI arises when external markets fail. |
| **Strategic behavior (FDI)** | FDI as an **imitative** move by oligopolists to match rivals and preserve competitive parity. |
| **Oligopoly** | Industry with a small number of interdependent competitors (e.g., Coca-Cola / Pepsi, Boeing / Airbus). |
| **Multipoint competition** | Rivals meeting each other across multiple country markets, which stabilizes the oligopoly. |
| **Eclectic Paradigm / OLI** | Dunning's framework: FDI when **O**wnership + **L**ocation + **I**nternalization advantages all hold. |
| **Ownership advantages (O)** | Firm-specific advantages (technology, brand, organizational culture) that rivals lack. |
| **Location-specific advantages (L)** | Advantages tied to a particular foreign location — resources, low-cost skilled labor, knowledge clusters, infrastructure — that the firm combines with its ownership advantages. |
| **Internalization advantages (I)** | It is more efficient to exploit the O advantages through internal control (FDI) than through contracts (licensing). |
| **Externalities / knowledge spillovers** | Spillover effects that benefit nearby firms — knowledge, skilled labor mobility, supplier networks. Textbook's preferred term: **externalities**. |
| **Cluster / nucleus** | Geographic concentration of firms in the same industry drawing on shared location-specific advantages and externalities. |

## 9. Watch out
- **Dunning did NOT win a Nobel Prize.** The transcript (and instructor) claim he did — this is factually wrong. The Eclectic Paradigm is enormously influential, but the Economics Nobel has never been awarded for the OLI framework. Do **not** repeat this on the exam.
- The instructor occasionally says **"Eclectic Paradise"** or "Eclectic Paradox" — the correct term is **"Eclectic Paradigm."**
- Use the instructor's **preferred phrases**: **"location-specific advantages"** and **"externalities"** (not just "spillovers"). Combining both phrases in answers is exam-critical.
- Spelling: **internalization**, not "internationalization" — different word.
- "**Knickerbocker**" — the theory is standard in Hill Ch. 8 but the transcript does not always name him; it is safe (and expected) to attribute the Strategic Behavior theory to Knickerbocker.
- OLI advantages must **all three** hold — Dunning is strict about this. If only O + I hold, the firm may still serve the market from home. Location is the decisive added dimension.
