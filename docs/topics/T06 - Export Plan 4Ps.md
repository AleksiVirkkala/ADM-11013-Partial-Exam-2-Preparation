# T06 — Export Plan (4Ps Framework)

**Spanish label:** Exportaciones — Plan de exportación
**Appears in lectures:** L18 (introduction of the 4Ps for the export plan), L19 (wine exercise — Baja California)
**Textbook:** Hill general marketing sections; Ch. 16 on exports (verify edition)
**Exam priority:** HIGH — teacher explicitly wants students able to "devise an export plan" from scratch using the 4Ps; the Baja California wine exercise is the canonical in-class illustration.

## 1. TL;DR
An **export plan** is built by answering four P-questions, adapted from classical marketing to the international-trade context: **Product** (what exactly is being shipped, in what spec, with what regulatory compliance and 8-digit HCC), **Promotion** (how to find foreign buyers and build enough trust to transact — including whether to use a **letter of credit**), **Price** (how the quoted price is built, mostly driven by the chosen **Incoterm** and payment terms), and **Physical distribution** (transport mode, freight forwarder, customs broker, bank involvement, route, warehousing, and the transport document — typically the **Bill of Lading**). The 4Ps framework is deliberately light; you fill it in with specifics for the product and destination market. For regulated goods such as wine, add sanitary/alcohol-regulatory certificates on top of the standard 5 trade documents.

## 2. The 4Ps for exports

### 2.1 Product
Define precisely **what is being exported** and what it must comply with in the destination country.
- **Specification**: product type, variety / model, vintage or batch, quality tier / market positioning.
- **Packaging**: bottle size, case count, pallet configuration, carton strength, temperature sensitivity (for wine: heat-sensitive — consider reefer container).
- **Origin / appellation**: geographic origin statement (matters for wine and other geographically indicated goods).
- **Labelling**: destination-country rules on language, alcohol content, allergens, health warnings, importer identification.
- **8-digit HCC (Harmonized Customs Code / *fracción arancelaria*)**: universal across countries; determines the tariff at destination. See [T07 — Export Documents and Flow](T07%20-%20Export%20Documents%20and%20Flow.md).
- **Regulatory compliance for regulated goods** (alcohol, food, pharma, agriculture): sanitary/phytosanitary certificates, alcohol-regulatory permits — **in addition to** the standard 5 trade documents.

### 2.2 Promotion
How to **find foreign buyers and build enough trust** to transact.
- **Target profile**: direct end-customer vs. **distributor/importer** vs. retailer vs. HoReCa (hotels/restaurants/catering). The channel choice cascades into pricing, volume commitments, and marketing control.
- **Finding buyers**: trade fairs (e.g. **Bancomext**-organised fairs in Mexico), product-specific tastings (for wine), importer networks, catalogues, online presence, references.
- **Trust-building on first sale**: for an unknown foreign counterparty, require a **letter of credit (LC)** so a bank guarantees payment on compliant documents. See [T09 — Trade Finance - LC and Factoring](T09%20-%20Trade%20Finance%20-%20LC%20and%20Factoring.md).
- **Payment terms offered**: prepayment, LC, documentary collection, open account — each with a different risk/cost trade-off.
- **Who pays at what stage**: advance deposit vs. payment on shipment vs. payment on delivery; tie this explicitly to the Incoterm chosen (Price P).

### 2.3 Price
The quoted export price is almost entirely driven by **which Incoterm is chosen** and what responsibilities it bundles.
- **Chosen Incoterm** (see [T08 — Incoterms](T08%20-%20Incoterms.md)): e.g. **FOB Ensenada** (buyer assumes freight & insurance from the port of departure), **CIF Rotterdam** (seller pays freight and minimum insurance to destination port, but risk still transfers at origin), **DAP buyer's warehouse** (seller delivers all the way to destination, buyer clears import), **DDP** (seller does everything including foreign duties).
- **What the quoted price includes**: product only, or product + freight, or product + freight + insurance, or full landed cost. The Incoterm answers this.
- **Currency**: USD, EUR, MXN — consider exchange-rate risk and which party bears it.
- **Margin** on top of cost of production + export-specific costs (freight, insurance, customs, broker fees, bank fees for LC).
- **Risk/cost split**: the Incoterm also fixes the **point of risk transfer** (Group C "trap": seller pays freight to destination but risk transfers at origin — buyer should add insurance).
- **Payment conditions**: prepayment / LC / open account — already chosen under Promotion but formally priced here.

### 2.4 Physical distribution
How the goods **physically and financially reach the buyer**.
- **Transport mode**: sea (container, most common for bulk wine), air (urgent/high-value), road (North America intra-regional), rail.
- **Freight forwarder** (arranges carriage end-to-end) and **customs broker / *agencia aduanal*** (prepares Pedimento, clears customs). See [T07 — Export Documents and Flow](T07%20-%20Export%20Documents%20and%20Flow.md).
- **Bank involvement**: for LC transactions, identify the **issuing bank** (buyer's) and **advising bank** (seller's). The bill of lading is the linchpin — the carrier won't release goods without it; the buyer only obtains it via the issuing bank after payment or credit arrangement. See [T09](T09%20-%20Trade%20Finance%20-%20LC%20and%20Factoring.md).
- **Shipping route and transit times**: port of departure → port of arrival → inland leg; estimate transit days.
- **Warehousing and handling**: temperature-controlled storage (wine), bonded warehouse at destination if needed, handover point (matches the Incoterm's named place).
- **Transport document**: the **Bill of Lading** (sea) or **Airway Bill** (air) — functions as (1) contract with carrier, (2) document of legal possession, (3) delivery receipt. This is one of the 5 required trade documents and is indispensable for the LC to work.

## 3. Worked example — Baja California wine producer

**Scenario (from L19):** You are the director of exports for a small Baja California winery. Build an export plan using the 4Ps and ensure the 5 required trade documents are in place. The teacher framed this as: why do Chilean, Argentine, Australian, European, and Californian wines flood foreign supermarkets while Mexican wine barely exports?

### 3.1 Product
- **Type**: premium red wine (e.g. a Nebbiolo or a Cabernet blend typical of the Valle de Guadalupe), specified varietal, vintage, and style; or the Coahuila reference *Casa Madero Tres Uvas* flagged in L18.
- **Packaging**: 750 ml glass bottles, 12 bottles/case, standard pallet configuration; cork vs. screw-cap per target-market preference.
- **Quality tier / positioning**: mid-to-premium; not competing on price with bulk Chilean/Argentine.
- **Origin / appellation**: Valle de Guadalupe, Baja California (use the origin as a selling point).
- **Labelling** for the destination country: local-language back label, alcohol content (%), sulphite/allergen disclosure, importer identification, health warnings (e.g. US TTB COLA, EU mandatory fields).
- **8-digit HCC**: wine-specific classification (HS chapter 22; verify the exact 8-digit *fracción arancelaria* for still wine of fresh grapes, ≤ 2 L containers).
- **Regulatory compliance**: alcoholic-beverage permits in destination country (e.g. US TTB, EU excise registration), plus any sanitary certificates. These are **extra** documents **on top of** the standard 5.

### 3.2 Promotion
- **Target profile**: a **specialised wine importer/distributor** in the destination market (not direct-to-retail — the winery lacks foreign sales infrastructure). HoReCa (high-end restaurants) is a realistic initial channel.
- **Finding the buyer**: Bancomext-organised wine fairs, international wine shows (e.g. ProWein), tastings hosted at Mexican consulates, targeted outreach to specialty importers, online presence with trade-facing materials.
- **First-sale trust**: require a **letter of credit** on the first transaction with any new foreign buyer — bank-guaranteed, irrevocable, conditional on compliant documents. This is the standard teacher-recommended de-risking move when parties don't know each other (see [T09](T09%20-%20Trade%20Finance%20-%20LC%20and%20Factoring.md)).
- **Payment terms**: LC at sight on first orders; move toward documentary collection or open account only once the relationship is established.

### 3.3 Price
- **Incoterm**: for the first export, quote **CIF destination port** (seller arranges and pays freight + minimum insurance to the port of arrival, so the buyer has price certainty up to the port). Alternatively **FOB Ensenada** if the importer has its own logistics in place and wants to control freight. Avoid **DDP** on a first deal — navigating foreign alcohol taxes and customs is hard for a small winery. See [T08 — Incoterms](T08%20-%20Incoterms.md).
- **What's included in the price**: under CIF — product + export clearance + freight + minimum insurance (110% of invoice value) to destination port. Import duties, local taxes, inland freight from port, and distribution are the buyer's cost.
- **Currency**: USD for North American buyers; EUR for European buyers.
- **Margin**: build up from ex-winery cost + bottling/packaging + inland freight to Ensenada + port/terminal handling + ocean freight + insurance + customs-broker fees + bank fees (LC issuance/amendment, documentary charges) + target margin.
- **Risk transfer (CIF)**: once goods cross the ship's rail at Ensenada — this is the Group C "trap". Tell the buyer explicitly that risk has transferred at origin even though seller paid freight to destination; the buyer should layer on broader cargo insurance.

### 3.4 Physical distribution
- **Transport mode**: ocean container (ideally a **reefer container** — wine is heat-sensitive); for small volumes, LCL (less-than-container-load) with a consolidator.
- **Freight forwarder**: book ocean freight Ensenada → destination port; negotiate reefer or standard dry container depending on route.
- **Customs broker (*agencia aduanal*)** in Mexico: prepares the **Pedimento** with the wine's 8-digit HCC; reviews the invoice, packing list, certificate of origin. See [T07](T07%20-%20Export%20Documents%20and%20Flow.md) for the 8-step flow.
- **Bank involvement**: issuing bank in buyer's country opens the LC; advising bank in Mexico authenticates it and forwards it to the winery; after shipment the winery presents LC-compliant documents (including the Bill of Lading) to the advising bank, which forwards to the issuing bank; issuing bank releases the Bill of Lading to the buyer only on payment. See [T09](T09%20-%20Trade%20Finance%20-%20LC%20and%20Factoring.md).
- **Route**: e.g. Ensenada → Long Beach (for US West Coast buyers) or Ensenada → Rotterdam via Panama (for European buyers).
- **Warehousing**: at destination, bonded warehouse until alcohol-tax duties are paid; temperature-controlled storage before onward distribution.
- **Transport document**: **Bill of Lading** (sea) — contract + proof of legal possession + delivery receipt. Without it the importer cannot take possession of the wine.

### 3.5 The required documents (from L19) for this shipment
1. Invoice (exporter)
2. Bill of Lading (shipping company)
3. Pedimento (customs broker)
4. Certificate of Origin (exporter — so the buyer can claim any FTA tariff preference)
5. Packing List (exporter)
6. **Product-specific**: alcoholic-beverage / sanitary certificates required at destination.

### 3.6 Additional considerations flagged in class (L18, L19)
- **Winery capacity**: can you supply the committed volume consistently? Over-committing kills first-time export relationships.
- **Competitive landscape**: Chilean, Argentine, Australian, European, and Californian wines dominate supermarkets — compete on origin/quality/story, not price.
- **Export credits**: Bancomext and similar agencies can provide export financing to smooth working capital.
- **Why Mexican wine is underrepresented abroad**: small production volumes, high domestic demand absorbing supply, lack of distributor relationships, lower international brand recognition vs. established New-World producers.

## 4. Key terminology
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **4Ps** | Marketing framework — **Product**, **Promotion**, **Price**, **Physical distribution** (a.k.a. Place / Logistics) — applied here to structuring an export plan. |
| **Product (4Ps)** | Specification of what is being exported: type, packaging, quality, origin, labelling, HCC, regulatory compliance. |
| **Promotion (4Ps)** | Marketing and channel strategy for finding foreign buyers and building trust; includes choice of distributor vs. direct, trade-fair presence, and payment-risk mitigation (e.g. LC). |
| **Price (4Ps)** | Export-price formation: driven primarily by the chosen Incoterm, plus currency, margin, and payment terms. |
| **Physical distribution (4Ps)** | Logistics and financial flow: transport mode, freight forwarder, customs broker, route, warehousing, bank involvement, transport document. |
| **Export plan** | Structured document that answers the four P-questions for a specific product and destination market, plus the five required trade documents. |
| **8-digit HCC / *fracción arancelaria*** | Harmonized Customs Code — universal product classification used at destination to determine tariff (see [T07](T07%20-%20Export%20Documents%20and%20Flow.md)). |
| **Bancomext** | Mexican foreign-trade bank; organises fairs and export-financing programs for Mexican exporters. |
| **HoReCa** | Hotels, Restaurants, Catering — a channel segment relevant for wine. |
| **Reefer container** | Refrigerated ocean container; used when temperature control is needed (e.g. wine in hot routes). |
| **Letter of Credit (LC)** | Irrevocable bank-guaranteed payment instrument; primary trust-builder on first-time export sales (see [T09](T09%20-%20Trade%20Finance%20-%20LC%20and%20Factoring.md)). |
| **Incoterm** | Three-letter ICC trade term (Incoterms 2020); defines who pays freight/insurance/duties and where risk transfers (see [T08](T08%20-%20Incoterms.md)). |
| **Bill of Lading** | Transport document with three functions: contract, legal possession, delivery receipt (see [T07](T07%20-%20Export%20Documents%20and%20Flow.md)). |
| **Valle de Guadalupe** | Main wine-producing region in Baja California; anchor of the class exercise. |
| **Casa Madero Tres Uvas** | Example Mexican wine (Coahuila) mentioned by the teacher in L18. |

## 7. Watch out
- **The 4Ps framework is deliberately light** — on the exam, fill it in with specifics for whatever case (wine, hinges, machinery, fruit) is given. A generic 4-bullet answer will underscore.
- **The 4th P has two names**: teacher uses **"Physical distribution"** (also *Place* / *Logistics*). Use the teacher's preferred label — *Physical distribution* — in exam answers.
- **Incoterm choice is a Price decision, not just a logistics decision.** The Incoterm determines what the quoted price covers. Link the two P's explicitly.
- **Group C Incoterms (CFR, CIF) carry a risk-transfer trap**: seller pays freight to destination, but risk transfers at origin. Mention this nuance when justifying the Incoterm choice.
- **Cross-link, don't re-explain**: Incoterms belong to T08, the LC flow to T09, the 5 documents / 8-step flow to T07. Here, reference those topics to justify 4P choices.
- **Wine has extra documents** (alcohol/sanitary) beyond the standard 5 — flag this explicitly in any wine-case answer.
- **On a first export sale, the teacher's default recipe** is: distributor channel + LC-backed payment + CIF (or FOB) Incoterm + Bill of Lading on a reefer container (if wine). Memorise this as a safe template.
