# T08 — Incoterms (E / F / C / D)

**Spanish label:** Incoterms (Facturación y pago)
**Appears in lectures:** L19, L22
**Textbook:** Hill Ch. 16 (exports)
**Exam priority:** HIGH — explicit Control Question ("differences among the main groups of Incoterms"); teacher will send an Incoterms video before the exam.

## 1. TL;DR
Incoterms (International Commercial Terms) are standardised three-letter codes published by the International Chamber of Commerce (ICC, Paris) that specify how **cost, risk, and responsibility** for freight, insurance, and duties are split between seller and buyer in an international sale-of-goods contract. They exist to **reduce legal ambiguity and disputes** across jurisdictions. There are 11 terms in the current **Incoterms 2020** revision, grouped into four categories — **E / F / C / D** — ordered from least to most seller responsibility. The exam bar is group-level understanding: know what each group means for cost and risk, especially that **C-group splits them** (seller pays freight to destination but risk transfers at origin).

## 2. Purpose of Incoterms
- Reduce **legal ambiguity** and disputes in international sales contracts — a shared cross-jurisdictional vocabulary.
- Pre-defined allocation of **cost, risk, and responsibility** between buyer and seller.
- Answer four practical questions for any international sale:
  1. Who arranges the freight?
  2. Who handles export / import clearance?
  3. Who pays import duties?
  4. **When does risk transfer from seller to buyer?** (central question)
- Published by the **ICC (Paris)**, originating in the 1930s, updated roughly every 10 years. Current version: **Incoterms 2020**.
- Every Incoterm on an invoice or contract **must be followed by a named place** (e.g., "FOB Shanghai Port Terminal 3", not just "FOB China") — otherwise the term is invalid.

## 3. The 4 groups

### E group — Departure (EXW)
- **Seller's minimum responsibility.** Seller only makes the goods available at own premises (the factory).
- Buyer handles everything onward: loading, export clearance, freight, insurance, import clearance, duties.
- Risk transfers at the **seller's premises**.
- Brutal for small buyers — the seller does not even load the truck. Used when the seller has no international-logistics capability or is very dominant in the deal.

### F group — Main carriage unpaid (FCA, FAS, FOB)
- Seller delivers the goods to a **carrier nominated by the buyer**, at the port of departure in the seller's country (example: Mexico → Europe, seller responsible up to the port of Veracruz).
- **Risk transfers at origin** once the goods are handed to the carrier.
- Buyer pays and arranges the main international carriage and insurance.
- **FOB** (Free On Board) is the classic marine case: risk passes when the goods cross the ship's rail / are loaded on the vessel at the origin port.
- Sub-variants distinguish whether the transfer point is on the wharf, alongside ship, at the ship's rail, or inside the vessel — teacher said he will **NOT** test these sub-variants.

### C group — Main carriage paid (CFR, CIF, CPT, CIP) — the TRAP
- Seller **pays the freight** (and insurance in CIF / CIP) to the named **destination port**.
- BUT **risk still transfers at origin** once the goods are handed to the carrier — the seller's payment of freight does not extend their risk coverage.
- → **Cost and risk are split**: this is the standard exam trap.
- **CFR** (Cost and Freight): seller pays freight, **no insurance** — if the ship sinks mid-voyage, the buyer bears the loss. Buyer should arrange separate insurance.
- **CIF** (Cost, Insurance and Freight): like CFR, but seller also pays **minimum insurance** (standard practice: 110 % of invoice value, standard risks). Smart buyers layer on broader insurance.
- **CPT / CIP** are the multimodal equivalents of CFR / CIF.

### D group — Arrival / delivered (DAP, DPU, DDP)
- Seller bears most of the cost and risk **all the way to the agreed destination** in the buyer's country (example: Freiburg im Breisgau, Germany).
- **DAP** (Delivered At Place) — seller delivers to the named destination; **buyer handles import customs, duties, taxes**.
- **DPU** (Delivered at Place Unloaded) — like DAP but seller also **unloads** at destination.
- **DDP** (Delivered Duty Paid) — seller handles **everything, including import customs clearance and duties**. Seller's **maximum / worst-case** responsibility. Use only when the seller has trusted agents or a subsidiary in the buyer's country.

## 4. Risk and cost transfer summary table

| Group | Cost to destination paid by | Risk transfers at | Insurance responsibility |
|-------|------------------------------|-------------------|--------------------------|
| **E (EXW)** | Buyer | Seller's premises | Buyer |
| **F (FOB)** | Buyer | Origin (goods loaded on ship) | Buyer |
| **C (CFR)** | Seller (freight only) | Origin | Buyer |
| **C (CIF)** | Seller (freight + insurance) | Origin | Seller (minimum cover, 110 % invoice) |
| **D (DAP)** | Seller (to destination) | Destination | Usually seller |
| **D (DDP)** | Seller (incl. import customs + duties) | Destination | Seller |

**Read the table as a sliding scale**: as you move E → F → C → D, seller responsibility grows. The **one discontinuity is the C group**, where *cost* moves toward the seller but *risk* stays at origin.

## 7. Polished sample answer (from L22 Control Questions)

*Q: What are the differences among the main groups of Incoterms?*

The main groups of Incoterms differ in how responsibilities, costs, and risks are divided between the exporter and the importer during transportation. In the **E group**, such as EXW, the seller has minimal responsibility and the buyer takes on almost all costs and risks from the seller's premises. In the **F group**, including FCA, FAS, and FOB, the seller delivers the goods to a carrier chosen by the buyer, after which the risk transfers to the buyer. In the **C group**, such as CFR, CIF, CPT, and CIP, the seller pays for transportation to the destination but the risk transfers to the buyer once the goods are handed over to the carrier, meaning **cost and risk are split**. In the **D group**, including DAP, DPU, and DDP, the seller bears most of the responsibility, cost, and risk by delivering the goods to the buyer's location, often including customs clearance in the most extreme case, **DDP**. Overall, the key difference between the groups is **how far along the transport chain the seller is responsible and at what point risk transfers to the buyer**.

## 9. Watch out
- **The C-group trap**: seller pays freight, buyer bears risk from origin — the most commonly tested subtlety. Under CFR, if the ship sinks the buyer loses.
- **CIF includes only minimum insurance** (110 % of invoice, standard risks) — a prudent buyer often buys additional cover.
- **FOB is a marine-only term.** "FOB Laredo, Texas" is a common Americas-region misuse. Never accept an invoice with just "FOB" and no named place — always insist on a precise location (port, terminal).
- **EXW** is the buyer's worst case; **DDP** is the seller's worst case. Use them only when the relationship and capabilities justify it.
- **Incoterms 2020** is the current version — revised some terms vs the 2010 edition (e.g., DAT was renamed DPU). Use 2020 references in the exam.
- Keep answers at **group level** — the teacher was explicit that he will not ask for sub-variant distinctions within a group.
