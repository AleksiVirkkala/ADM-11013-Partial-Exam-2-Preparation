# T02 — NAFTA and TMEC / USMCA

**Spanish label:** TLCAN / T-MEC
**Appears in lectures:** L17, L19, L22
**Textbook:** Hill Ch. 9 regional economic integration; Ch. 7 instruments of trade policy / tariffs (verify edition)
**Exam priority:** HIGH — teacher said TMEC topics are central to this partial exam and explicitly flagged rules of origin, the tariff-removal schedule, Maquiladora, and TMEC revisions

## 1. TL;DR
NAFTA entered into force 1 January 1994 with 22 chapters and the core objectives of eliminating trade barriers, protecting intellectual property, increasing investment, promoting free and just trade, and facilitating dispute resolution. Every tradable good is identified by the same 8-digit Harmonized Customs Code, and each code was negotiated country-by-country into one of four tariff-removal categories (A immediate, B 5 years, C 10 years, C+ 15 years to 2008). Rules of origin stop third-country "trampoline" exports by requiring a minimum Regional Value Content — 60% under the Transaction Value (FOB) formula or 50% under the Net Cost formula. The 2018 renegotiation renamed NAFTA to T-MEC / USMCA and raised the automotive RVC threshold (teacher said "higher percentage"; commonly published figure 75%). The Maquiladora program (1965) was a pre-NAFTA assembly exception in Mexican border cities that charged US tariff only on Mexican value added; it is now largely obsolete under T-MEC.

## 2. NAFTA essentials
- **Entered into force:** 1 January 1994. Renamed **USMCA** (English) / **T-MEC** (Spanish) in the 2018 renegotiation; teacher still often says "NAFTA" in class.
- **Structure:** 22 chapters.
- **Objectives (memorise the five):**
  1. Eliminate trade barriers (tariff and non-tariff).
  2. Protect intellectual property rights.
  3. Increase investment opportunities.
  4. Promote free and just trade.
  5. Facilitate resolution of controversies (dispute resolution).
- **Core principle:** regionally produced products must be treated the same as nationally produced products (national treatment inside the bloc).
- **Outcome:** barriers were vastly reduced, not fully eliminated.
- **Nature of the agreement:** it is a Free Trade Agreement (each country keeps its own external tariff against third countries such as China) — **not** a Customs Union. This FTA-vs-Customs-Union distinction is one of the teacher's flagged basic exam questions.

## 3. Tariff categories (A / B / C / C+)
Every 8-digit customs code was assigned, **separately for each of the three countries**, to one of four removal schedules. The more sensitive the good to the domestic economy, the slower the removal. The same good can be category A in one country and C in another.

| Category | Removal schedule | Final year |
|---|---|---|
| **A** | Tariff removed immediately on 1 Jan 1994 | 1994 |
| **B** | Gradual over 5 years (1/5 per year) | 1 Jan 1998 |
| **C** | Gradual over 10 years (1/10 per year) | 1 Jan 2004 |
| **C+** | Gradual over 15 years — most sensitive products (corn, beans, automobiles, textiles) | 1 Jan 2008 |

**Mexico's 5 tariff levels** on goods from the USA/Canada: 0%, 5%, 10%, 15%, 20%. A category-B starting tariff decrements by the same proportion each year until it reaches 0 on 1 Jan 1998.

**Political demagoguery note.** In 2008 López Obrador claimed the final removal of C+ tariffs on corn/beans/autos would wreck Mexican agriculture and industry. The flaw: those tariffs had already been phased down 1/15 per year for 15 years — on 1 Jan 2008 only the last 1/15 was removed.

## 5. Rules of origin — two methods
**Purpose.** Prevent third-country firms from using a member country as a **trampoline**. Example: a Korean firm ships socks to a California warehouse, relabels them "Made in USA," then re-exports tariff-free to Mexico under NAFTA. Rules of origin block this by requiring a minimum share of regional value (Regional Value Content, **RVC**).

**Expertise.** The customs broker (and the customs officer) is the specialist who applies these formulas.

### Transaction Value method (Method 1, FOB basis)

```
RVC = (TV − VNM) / TV × 100
```

- **Threshold: > 60%** → the good qualifies as regional and enters tariff-free.
- `TV` = Transaction Value, on an **FOB** (Free On Board) basis — i.e., *excluding* international insurance and freight.
- `VNM` = Value of Non-originating Materials (inputs sourced from outside the FTA).

### Net Cost method (Method 2)

```
RVC = (NC − VNM) / NC × 100
```

- **Threshold: > 50%** → qualifies as regional. Used when the FOB Transaction Value formula fails.
- `NC` = Net Cost of producing the good (excludes freight and insurance).
- `VNM` = as above.

## 6. Harmonized Customs Code (HCC / HS)
- Every tradable good has an **8-digit Harmonized Customs Code**. "Everything except live humans has a customs code" (a cadaver has one).
- The code is **the same in every country** — this universality is what makes international tariff lookup possible.
- The first digits identify broad categories (e.g., oils/fats of animal or vegetable origin), then further digits identify sub-parts.
- To determine the tariff on a good imported under NAFTA/USMCA you need the 8-digit HS code (plus any negotiated exceptions). This is the teacher's flagged basic exam question: *"what information is necessary to determine a tariff on a good imported under NAFTA?"* → the 8-digit harmonized customs code.
- **Important caveat.** The HS code itself is universal, but the **tariff-removal category** (A, B, C, C+) attached to that code can differ between partner countries because categories are negotiated country-by-country.
- The **customs broker** is the licensed specialist who looks up the code, prepares the Pedimento, applies rules of origin, and pays duties on the importer's behalf. Errors (e.g., wrong customs code) can cost the broker their licence.

## 7. TMEC / USMCA — key revisions vs NAFTA
The 2018 renegotiation under the Trump administration renamed the agreement **USMCA** (in English) / **T-MEC** (in Spanish) and adjusted several rules. Teacher-flagged exam item.

- **Automotive regional content raised.** The RVC threshold for automobiles was raised above the general 60%/50% rules. Teacher stated "higher percentage" without pinning a number; the commonly published figure is **75% for autos** — confirm before citing verbatim. This was a response to Trump's (disputed) claim that Mexico was assembling Chinese parts and re-exporting as "Made in Mexico."
- **General RVC framework preserved.** The two formulas (Transaction Value at 60%, Net Cost at 50%) continue to apply to non-automotive goods.
- **Tariff categories (A / B / C / C+) are historical.** All phase-outs completed by 2008 (final C+ step); the renegotiation did not reopen the general tariff-removal schedule.
- **Continuing context the teacher flagged as "coming in more detail":** T-MEC's table of contents and recent changes were foreshadowed as a dedicated session — expect additional updates beyond the automotive RVC.

## 8. Maquiladora program
- **Origin: 1965.** Pre-NAFTA exception that allowed **foreign inputs to be imported duty-free into Mexican border cities** for assembly and then re-exported.
- **Tariff mechanism.** When the assembled good crossed back into the US, US tariff was assessed **only on the Mexican value added** (mostly labour — historically predominantly female labour in border-city assembly plants), not on the full product value.
- **Historical role.** Anchored the border-city industrial corridor (Tijuana, Ciudad Juárez, Reynosa, etc.); drove decades of US-Mexico assembly integration in electronics, textiles, and auto parts.
- **Current relevance.** Largely **obsolete under NAFTA/T-MEC**, because the rules of origin framework and the general tariff elimination supersede the specific Maquiladora exception for most flows. The term and some industrial-policy residue persist, but the original tariff advantage has been absorbed into the broader FTA regime.

## 9. Key terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| NAFTA / USMCA / T-MEC | North American Free Trade Agreement (1994, 22 chapters); renamed USMCA (English) / T-MEC (Spanish) in 2018. |
| 8-digit Harmonized Customs Code (HCC / HS) | Internationally standardised classification identifying any tradable good; identical across all countries; required to determine a tariff. |
| Category A / B / C / C+ | NAFTA tariff-removal schedules: immediate / 5-year / 10-year / 15-year (to 2008). Negotiated country-by-country per code. |
| FTA | Free Trade Agreement — tariffs removed among members; each country keeps its own external tariff against third parties. NAFTA/USMCA is an FTA. |
| Customs Union | FTA + harmonized **external** tariffs against third countries. NAFTA/USMCA is **not** a Customs Union. |
| Rules of Origin | Criteria determining whether a good qualifies as regional, preventing third-country trampoline exports. |
| Trampoline export | Evasion pattern where a third-country good is routed through a member country to gain tariff-free access; blocked by rules of origin. |
| RVC (Regional Value Content) | Percentage of a good's value originating within the FTA region. |
| TV (Transaction Value) | Invoice value of the good on an FOB basis (excl. international insurance and freight). |
| NC (Net Cost) | Cost of producing the good, excluding freight and insurance. |
| VNM | Value of Non-originating Materials (inputs sourced from outside the FTA). |
| FOB (Free On Board) | Incoterm; price excludes international insurance and freight. Used in the Transaction Value RVC formula. |
| Customs broker | Licensed specialist who prepares the Pedimento, applies rules of origin, and pays duties on behalf of the importer. |
| Maquiladora | 1965 program allowing duty-free import of foreign inputs into Mexican border cities for assembly; US tariff charged only on Mexican value added; largely obsolete under T-MEC. |

## 10. Formulas

**Regional Value Content — Transaction Value method (FOB basis):**

```
RVC = (TV − VNM) / TV × 100          threshold: > 60%
```

**Regional Value Content — Net Cost method:**

```
RVC = (NC − VNM) / NC × 100          threshold: > 50%
```

**Automotive exception under T-MEC/USMCA (2018):** higher RVC threshold, commonly cited as **75%** (verify).

## 12. Worked examples

### Category-B phase-out, starting tariff 20%, over 5 years (1/5 removed per year)

| Year | Tariff |
|------|--------|
| 1994 | 16% |
| 1995 | 12% |
| 1996 | 8% |
| 1997 | 4% |
| 1998 | 0% |

### RVC — Transaction Value method

Socks exported from the US to Mexico: `TV = 100` (FOB), `VNM = 35` (imported Korean yarn and trim).

```
RVC = (100 − 35) / 100 × 100 = 65%
```

65% > 60% threshold → the socks qualify as regional and enter Mexico tariff-free under NAFTA/USMCA.

### RVC — Net Cost method

Same good, `NC = 80`, `VNM = 35`:

```
RVC = (80 − 35) / 80 × 100 = 56.25%
```

56.25% > 50% threshold → the good qualifies via the Net Cost method even if it had failed the 60% Transaction Value test.
