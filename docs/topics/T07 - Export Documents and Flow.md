# T07 — Export Documents and the 8-Step Flow

**Spanish label:** Documentos de exportación; Flujo de operación de comercio exterior
**Appears in lectures:** L17, L18, L19
**Textbook:** Hill Ch. 16 (exports, export documentation); course supplementary material (Pedimento walk-through, Primex Laguna / Siemens example)
**Exam priority:** HIGH — teacher explicitly flagged the 5 documents, the 3 purposes of the Bill of Lading, the 8-step flow, and the 8-digit HCC as exam-certain. Quote: *"this would be a wonderful question on the 2nd exam."*

## 1. TL;DR
Every international trade transaction requires **5 documents**: Pedimento, Certificate of Origin, Packing List, Invoice, and Bill of Lading. The customs broker (*agente aduanal*) prepares the **Pedimento** around the **8-digit Harmonized Customs Code (HCC)**, which is identical worldwide and is the key input for determining the tariff. The exporter prepares the Certificate of Origin, Packing List, and Invoice. The transport company issues the **Bill of Lading**, which has three legal functions: **title, contract, and delivery receipt**. The whole operation unfolds in **8 steps**, starting with 4Ps negotiation and ending with physical customs inspection in the importing country.

## 2. The 5 required documents

Memorization mnemonic: **"PC-PIB"** → **P**edimento, **C**ert. of Origin, **P**acking List, **I**nvoice, **B**ill of Lading.
Or, ordered as the teacher lists it (L19): **I-B-P-C-P** → Invoice, Bill of Lading, Pedimento, Certificate of Origin, Packing List.

### 2.1 Pedimento
- **Who prepares:** Customs broker (*agente aduanal* / Agencia Aduanal) — or, if none is hired, an in-house employee assigned the role.
- **What it is:** The Mexican government import/export **tax declaration**. Without a Pedimento stamped as paid, the goods cannot leave customs.
- **Contains:** the 8-digit **Harmonized Customs Code** (*fracción arancelaria*), duties calculation (IGI), VAT (IVA 16%), DTA, prevalidation fee (PRV), exchange rate, point of entry/exit (aduana), mode of transport, gross weight, Incoterm, supplier data, invoice references, and consolidated shipment lines.
- **Role in the flow:** The broker prepares it in step 3. In step 7 the broker pays duties and receives the stamp. In step 8 customs uses it to compare declared vs. physical goods.
- **Criticality:** Errors (e.g., wrong HCC) can cost the broker their license.

### 2.2 Certificate of Origin
- **Who prepares:** Exporter.
- **What it attests:** That the goods qualify as *originating* under a free-trade agreement (e.g., T-MEC / USMCA), based on the **Rules of Origin** — i.e., enough regional value content. This is what unlocks preferential (zero) tariffs under an FTA.
- **Role in the flow:** Prepared in step 4 alongside Packing List and Invoice; sent to importer and importer's broker.

### 2.3 Packing List
- **Who prepares:** Exporter.
- **Purpose:** Itemizes what is physically in each box/package/pallet. Used by customs to verify that the physical goods match the documentation (step 8) and by the importer to receive and unload the shipment.

### 2.4 Invoice (*factura*)
- **Who prepares:** Exporter / seller.
- **Purpose:** The commercial invoice — the sales document. States prices, quantities, currency, payment terms, and the chosen **Incoterm** (which is what ties the invoice to the downstream cost/risk allocation — see [T08](T08%20-%20Incoterms.md)).
- **Must match** the supplier data on the Pedimento — mismatches are a problem.

### 2.5 Bill of Lading (*guía* / *conocimiento de embarque*)
- **Who prepares:** Shipping company / carrier / transport firm. Issued to the exporter/shipper. (Course materials sometimes phrase this as "exporter / transport firm" — in practice the **carrier issues it** and the exporter supplies particulars.)
- **Air-freight equivalent:** **Airway Bill** (same three legal functions).
- **Three purposes** (MEMORIZE — explicit exam item):
  1. **Document of legal possession / title** — proves right to possess the goods (the "not a pirate on the high seas" test; analogous to a car title).
  2. **Contract** between shipper (exporter) and the transport / shipping company.
  3. **Delivery receipt** — the importer must present a copy to the carrier to take possession at destination.
- **Why it matters beyond transport:** The Bill of Lading is the linchpin of the Letter of Credit mechanism — the issuing bank holds it until the buyer pays, which is what makes the LC enforceable (see [T09](T09%20-%20Trade%20Finance%20-%20LC%20and%20Factoring.md)).

### Note: product-specific extra documents
Depending on the good, additional documents are required beyond the 5:
- **Food / fruit / animal products:** sanitary certificate.
- **Plants, seeds, produce:** phytosanitary certificate.
- **Alcohol (e.g., wine):** destination-country alcohol labeling compliance (alcohol content, health warnings, language rules).
- **Pharmaceuticals, hazmat, textiles, etc.:** product-specific regulatory certificates.

## 3. The 8-digit Harmonized Customs Code (HCC)

- **Spanish:** *fracción arancelaria*.
- **Structure:** 8 digits. The **first 6 digits are strictly global** under the Harmonized System (HS); the **7th–8th digits are country-level** sub-classifications. Taken together, the 8-digit code is treated in class as "**identical in every country**" and is the element that unambiguously identifies what the good is.
- **Coverage:** "Everything has a code." Even a cadaver has one. Only *live humans* do not.
- **Why it matters:** It is the key input to determine the **tariff** on an imported/exported good. Before you can know the tariff, you must know the HCC.
- **Important nuance:** While the **HCC itself is universal**, the **tariff-removal category** (A / B / C / C+ under T-MEC) assigned to that code can differ across the three countries — each country negotiated its own schedule. (See [T02 — NAFTA and TMEC](T02%20-%20NAFTA%20and%20TMEC.md).)
- **Expert:** The **customs broker** is the specialist who knows how to look up and assign the correct HCC.
- **Example from class:** HCC `83021099` = metal mountings / fittings (= *bisagras* / hinges) in the Primex Laguna / Outgo Diseño Pedimento walk-through.

## 4. The 8-step flow of a trade transaction

Teacher quote: *"I'm going to include something like this on the test."*

| Step | Action | Who does it |
|------|--------|-------------|
| 1 | **Negotiation** between importer and exporter around the **4Ps** (Product, Price, Place/physical distribution, Promotion/publicity) and contract terms. Clients found via Bancomext fairs, trade fairs, catalogues, internet. | Importer + Exporter |
| 2 | **Choose a customs agent** (external broker / Agencia Aduanal) or assign an internal employee to facilitate. | Importing (Mexican) party |
| 3 | **Prepare the Pedimento** and review it with the client — the core tax declaration built around the HCC. | Customs broker |
| 4 | **Prepare Certificate of Origin, Packing List, and Invoice**; send copies to the importer and the importer's customs agent. | Exporter |
| 5 | **Issue the Bill of Lading** with its three legal functions. | Shipping / transport company (to the exporter) |
| 6 | **Ship the merchandise** to customs of the importing country. | Carrier |
| 7 | **Pay duties and taxes** (IGI, IVA, DTA, PRV) to Customs; Pedimento is **stamped** as proof of payment. Without the stamp, the goods cannot leave customs. | Customs broker (on behalf of client) |
| 8 | **Customs inspection** — the importing country's customs **physically compares the merchandise against the import documents** (Pedimento, Certificate of Origin, Packing List, Invoice, Bill of Lading). | Customs authority (importing country) |

Memorization acronym: **N-C-P-E-B-S-D-I**
→ **N**egotiate · **C**hoose broker · **P**edimento · **E**xporter docs · **B**ill of Lading · **S**hip · **D**uties paid/stamped · **I**nspection.

## 5. Key terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Pedimento | Mexican import/export tax declaration; prepared by customs broker; built around the HCC. |
| HCC / *fracción arancelaria* | 8-digit Harmonized Customs Code; same across countries; identifies the good and drives the tariff. |
| Customs broker / *agente aduanal* / Agencia Aduanal | Licensed private-sector specialist who prepares the Pedimento, applies Rules of Origin, and pays duties. |
| Customs / *Aduana* | The government authority (distinct from the broker). |
| Certificate of Origin | Exporter-issued document; certifies FTA-qualifying origin via Rules of Origin. |
| Packing List | Exporter-issued itemization of each box/package. |
| Invoice / *factura* | Commercial invoice from the exporter; includes prices, terms, and chosen Incoterm. |
| Bill of Lading / *guía* / *conocimiento de embarque* | Transport document with 3 legal purposes (title, contract, receipt); issued by the carrier. |
| Airway Bill | Air-freight equivalent of the Bill of Lading; same three functions. |
| IGI | *Impuesto General de Importación* — import duty. Under T-MEC = 0% for qualifying goods. |
| IVA | *Impuesto al Valor Agregado* — Mexican VAT, 16%; still charged even when IGI = 0. |
| DTA | *Derecho de Trámite Aduanero* — customs-processing fee. |
| PRV / *Prevalidación* | Pre-validation fee on the Pedimento. |
| Régimen A1 | Definitive import regime (goods are not returning to origin country). |
| 4Ps | Product, Price, Place (physical distribution / logistics), Promotion/Publicity — framework used in step 1. |
| Bancomext | Mexican foreign trade bank; organizes fairs to connect Mexican exporters with foreign buyers. |

## 7. Worked mini-cases

### 7.1 Baja California wine export (ties into [T06](T06%20-%20Export%20Plan%204Ps.md))
The 5 mandatory documents apply as usual. **Beyond** them, a wine shipment will require:
- **Sanitary / alcohol-regulatory certificate** from the destination country.
- **Destination-country labeling compliance**: alcohol content, health warnings, language, allergen disclosure.
- The **Certificate of Origin** is what lets the importer claim FTA preferential tariffs (e.g., under a Mexico–EU or Mexico–Japan FTA).
- The **Incoterm** on the Invoice (e.g., FOB Ensenada vs. CIF Rotterdam vs. DAP buyer warehouse) drives who pays for temperature-controlled freight and insurance — critical for wine (see [T08](T08%20-%20Incoterms.md)).

### 7.2 Primex Laguna / Outgo Diseño (2022 import, from class)
- Importer: *Outgo Diseño y Manufactura* (Gómez Palacio, Durango).
- Broker: Primex Laguna S.C.
- Aduana: Nuevo Laredo, Tamaulipas.
- Consolidated suppliers (Siemens Mobility OR; Superior Metal Products IL; Wilson Tool MN) staged in Laredo TX.
- HCC: `83021099` (metal fittings — *bisagras*).
- FX: ≈ 20.66 MXN/USD (May 2022).
- Taxes: IGI = 0 (T-MEC); IVA ≈ 22,228 MXN at 16%; paid at BBVA branch beside customs. Total charge ≈ MXN 34,000.
- This illustrates how a **single Pedimento consolidates multiple suppliers**, uses the HCC as its key field, and is only released on paid-stamp.

## 9. Watch out

- **Who prepares the Bill of Lading:** course materials say "exporter / transport firm," but the **carrier issues it** and the exporter provides particulars. Either phrasing is defensible on the exam; the **three functions are the critical part**.
- **HCC "universality":** strictly, only the **first 6 digits** are globally harmonized under the HS; the **7th–8th are country-level**. The teacher collapses this to "the 8-digit code is the same in every country" — use the teacher's framing on the exam.
- **IGI = 0 ≠ no taxes.** Under T-MEC, duties are zero for qualifying goods, but **VAT (IVA 16%)** is still charged on imports — and DTA / PRV on top.
- **Tariff category (A/B/C/C+) is per-country**, even though the HCC is global. "Category A in Mexico = Category A in US/Canada" is **False**.
- **"FOB" without a named place is invalid** — an Incoterm on the Invoice must always be followed by a named place (e.g., "FOB Laredo, TX"). This is a common Americas-region misuse (see [T08](T08%20-%20Incoterms.md)).
- **Product-specific documents are *additional*, not a substitute**: e.g., a wine shipment still needs all 5 mandatory documents **plus** alcohol labeling compliance.
- **Pedimento errors are career-ending for the broker** — wrong HCC can cost the broker their license, which is why step 3's "review with client" matters.
