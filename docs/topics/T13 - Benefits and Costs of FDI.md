# T13 — Benefits and Costs of FDI (Home vs Host Country)

**Appears in lectures:** L20 (FDI entry frame / partial table in notes), L21 (host-country benefits spoken in class; other quadrants cut off by end of class), L22 (take-home Control Question 2 — my full four-quadrant draft)
**Textbook:** Hill Ch. 8, "Benefits and Costs of FDI" — p. 237 onwards (lecturer's print edition; p. 255 in online edition — use section heading rather than page number)
**Exam priority:** HIGH — flagged `[EXAM]` in **L21**: "structure answer in four blocks: benefits to host, benefits to home, costs to host, costs to home." Also appears as L22 Control Question 2 (take-home participation exercise, *not* itself an exam flag).

## 1. TL;DR
FDI produces a four-quadrant balance sheet: the **host country** gains capital, technology, jobs, BoP improvements, and stronger competition, but pays in possible loss of competition, profit-repatriation BoP drag, and erosion of national sovereignty. The **home country** gains from repatriated profits, input-supplier spillovers, and reverse knowledge transfer, but absorbs the initial capital outflow, risks long-term trade deficits if production migrates, and may lose domestic jobs. On net, host countries *usually* come out positive — this is why Mexico actively courts ≈ USD 35–40 billion of inward FDI per year — but the costs are real and politically charged.

## 3. Host country — benefits (in detail)

### 3.1 Resource transfer effects
- Foreign firm brings **capital + technology + managerial know-how** into the host economy.
- Raises productivity and growth; fills capital and skill gaps the domestic economy cannot fill on its own.
- Lecturer's anchor: Mexico receives **≈ USD 38 billion of new FDI per year**; manufacturing capacity is physically built "down" in Mexico by foreign investors.

### 3.2 Employment effects
- **Direct** jobs in the subsidiary itself.
- **Indirect** jobs in local supply chains, logistics, and services that feed the subsidiary.
- Canonical Mexican example: the **automobile cluster** — "tons of new investments, automobiles, manufacturing of all types" (L21).

### 3.3 Balance-of-payments effects
- Initial FDI inflow is a **credit on the capital account**.
- Current account improves when the subsidiary's output either
  - **substitutes for imports** (FDI produces locally what was previously imported), or
  - **generates exports** (the subsidiary exports to third countries or back to the home country).

### 3.4 Effect on competition and innovation
- Entry of a foreign MNC **breaks local oligopolies / monopolies**.
- Typically **lowers prices and raises quality** for consumers.
- **Stimulates innovation** — domestic incumbents must upgrade to keep up.

## 4. Host country — costs

### 4.1 Adverse effects on competition
- A dominant foreign MNC can **crush local firms** that cannot match its scale, technology, or marketing budget.
- Result: *reduced* competition in the long run, the opposite of the competition benefit above.
- Mexico-specific concern flagged in class: **growing Chinese FDI** taking over some markets.

### 4.2 Adverse balance-of-payments effects
- **Profit repatriation** — dividends flow *out* from the subsidiary to the foreign parent (debit on the current account).
- **Imports of inputs / components** — if the subsidiary sources capital goods or parts from the parent or third countries, this is another BoP outflow.
- Net BoP effect can turn negative once the initial inflow is amortised.

### 4.3 National sovereignty / autonomy concerns
- Key economic decisions (investment, sourcing, layoffs, plant closures) are made at a **foreign headquarters**, not in the host country.
- Risk of **conflict between foreign-firm interests and host-country interests** in strategic sectors.
- Politically sensitive — feeds populist/protectionist reactions.

## 5. Home country — benefits

### 5.1 Balance of payments from repatriated profits
- **Dividends and royalties** from the foreign subsidiary flow back to the parent company.
- This is a **credit on the home-country current account** — the mirror image of the host country's repatriation cost.

### 5.2 Employment spillovers
- Home-country firms often **supply inputs, components, and services** to the foreign subsidiary.
- Those supplier orders create / preserve jobs back home.
- Subsidiary may also generate **demand for home-country exports** (capital equipment, branded inputs).

### 5.3 Reverse knowledge transfer
- The MNE **learns from operating in the foreign market** — consumer tastes, new processes, local management practices.
- These capabilities can be **brought back to the home country** and applied to other operations.

## 6. Home country — costs

### 6.1 Initial capital outflow
- The FDI itself is a **capital outflow** from the home economy when the investment is made.
- **Worsens home-country BoP in the short term** until profits start flowing back.

### 6.2 Long-term trade deficits
- If the foreign subsidiary's output **substitutes for what the home country used to export**, the home country loses export revenue.
- Can produce a **persistent trade deficit** in the affected sector.

### 6.3 Job losses / offshoring
- When FDI is a **substitute for domestic production** (rather than a complement), jobs relocate to the lower-cost host country.
- Politically the most visible cost — drives much of the backlash against outward FDI in developed economies.

## 7. Key terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **FDI (Foreign Direct Investment)** | Equity investment giving the investor **control** (≥ 10 % of capital) over a foreign operation. |
| **Host country** | The country *receiving* the FDI (where the subsidiary operates). |
| **Home country** | The country where the *investing* firm is headquartered. |
| **BoP (Balance of Payments)** | Accounting record of a country's transactions with the rest of the world; split into current account (trade, income) and capital account (investment flows). |
| **Repatriation** | Moving profits, dividends, or royalties from a foreign subsidiary back to the parent company in the home country. |
| **Import substitution** | Domestic production replacing previously-imported goods; improves the host's current account. |
| **Resource-transfer effect** | Inflow of capital, technology, and management skills brought in by the foreign investor. |
| **Offshoring** | Relocating production from the home country to a lower-cost foreign location, typically via FDI. |
| **Spillover** | Indirect benefit (or cost) that diffuses from the FDI to the wider host or home economy (jobs, knowledge, suppliers). |

## 9. Worked example — Mexico as host country
- **Inflows:** ≈ USD 35–40 billion of new FDI per year (teacher's figure in L21).
- **Dominant sectors:** automobiles and auto parts, electronics, maquiladora assembly.
- **Benefits realised:** employment (northern-border auto cluster), capital, technology transfer, export generation back into the US market (USMCA rules of origin reward this).
- **Costs experienced:** profit repatriation to US/European/Asian parents, deep dependency on US-based MNCs, sovereignty concerns when strategic sectors (energy, telecoms) are foreign-owned, and — recently — **growing Chinese FDI** seen as displacing local producers.
- **Exam-answer posture:** Mexico is a textbook case where the host-country benefits *on balance* outweigh the costs, but the costs are politically salient enough to drive policy (nearshoring, content requirements, energy-sector reservations).
