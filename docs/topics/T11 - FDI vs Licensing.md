## 1. TL;DR

Licensing is cheap and low-risk, but it fails precisely when the firm has something worth protecting. A licensee can walk off with the know-how, the licensor cannot tightly control how its product is produced, marketed, or priced, and many of a firm's real advantages — managerial practices, manufacturing discipline, organisational culture — simply cannot be written into a contract. When any of these three conditions bite, **FDI dominates licensing** because ownership **internalises** value that a contract cannot secure (internalization / market-imperfections theory, see [T12](T12%20-%20FDI%20Theories.md)). Three industry contexts make licensing especially inappropriate: high-tech, global oligopolies, and intense cost-pressure industries. Figure 8.4 is the canonical decision tree that operationalises this logic.

## 2. The 3 weaknesses of licensing (memorize — verbatim)

> These are the three bullets to write on the exam. Keep them short and enumerated.

### 2.1 Licensing may give away valuable technological know-how to a potential foreign competitor
- The licensee absorbs the technology and can become a **future rival** once the contract expires (or even in breach of it).
- Especially dangerous for patents, trade secrets, and **process know-how** that is hard to police at arm's length.
- Textbook illustration: **Matsushita → Sony** (technology leaking to what later becomes a competitor).
- Illustrative classroom example: a European firm licensing to a Chinese partner worries about IP enforcement.

### 2.2 Licensing does not give the firm tight control over production, marketing, and strategy
- No direct oversight of how the licensee produces, prices, promotes, or positions the product.
- The licensor loses the ability to **coordinate across markets** (e.g. to exploit **factor-cost differences** by producing only part of the product in a given country — a Maquiladora-style split a licensee would be unlikely to accept).
- Risk of **sub-optimal profitability** and brand damage in the foreign market.

### 2.3 Competitive advantages based on management / marketing / manufacturing capabilities are not amenable to licensing
- If the firm's edge lies in **tacit, firm-specific capabilities** (organisational routines, manufacturing discipline, corporate culture, service standards), these **cannot be packaged into a licence contract**.
- A licensee may be unable to replicate them even in good faith.
- Cultural example from class: **Toyota / Japanese firms** famously avoid licensing — the belief is that only their own employees can fully run the system (the "Toyota Way" is not contractable).

**Textbook summary rule (p. 238, last paragraph):** Licensing is inadequate when (a) valuable know-how cannot be adequately protected by a licensing contract, (b) tight control over the foreign entity is needed to maximise market share, or (c) the firm's skills/know-how are not amenable to licensing.

## 3. Industries where licensing is ESPECIALLY bad

From Hill Ch. 8, *Focus on Managerial Implications* (p. 244 / p. 253). Three industry contexts make licensing uniquely inappropriate.

### 3.1 High-technology industries
- IP protection is paramount; leaking firm-specific expertise is existential.
- Licensing is **hazardous** because once the know-how is out, it cannot be recovered.
- Ownership (FDI) keeps the R&D and production under one roof.

### 3.2 Global oligopolies
- A small number of multinational rivals are **competitively interdependent**: a move by one triggers a reaction by another.
- The firm must maintain **tight control over foreign operations** to launch **coordinated attacks** against global rivals across markets (multipoint competition).
- Canonical exam example: **Coca-Cola vs. Pepsi** (teacher's chosen illustration). Also: **Boeing vs. Airbus**.
- A licensee would not take orders in a coordinated global contest.

### 3.3 Industries under intense cost pressure
- The firm must keep **tight control over global production locations** to disperse activity to the **most favourable factor-cost locations**, minimising cost and maximising value.
- With thin margins, "you can lose it easily" (teacher) if a licensee deviates from the cost plan.
- A licensee cannot be trusted to optimise the global cost footprint on the licensor's behalf.

## 4. Figure 8.4 (Hill) — decision framework for FDI vs Licensing vs Exporting

Cascading decision tree. Walk it in order:

1. **Are transport costs and tariffs low?**
   - **Yes → Export.**
   - No → go to 2.
2. **Is know-how amenable to licensing?** (i.e. simple, codifiable — pizza recipe rather than semiconductor process)
   - **No → FDI.**
   - Yes → go to 3.
3. **Is tight control over foreign operations required?** (strategic coordination, quality, cost)
   - **Yes → FDI.**
   - No → go to 4.
4. **Can know-how be adequately protected by a licensing contract?**
   - **No → FDI.**
   - **Yes → License.**

**Reading of the tree:** FDI wins whenever any of the three weaknesses (§2) are binding. Licensing survives only when know-how is codifiable *and* loose control is acceptable *and* contracts can protect the IP. Classic successful-licensing case: **pizza / McDonald's-style franchising** — operations manual travels well, brand is in the consumer's head, tight control is not required.

*Figure-number caveat:* instructor's edition numbers this **Figure 8.4**; some editions of Hill number it **8.1**. Verify against your copy.

## 5. General case for FDI over licensing

When licensing's three weaknesses bite, FDI wins because the firm:

- **Retains control** over production, marketing, and strategy in the foreign market.
- **Captures full profit** rather than a licence fee / royalty.
- **Protects know-how** by keeping it inside the firm (no transfer to an external party that could become a rival).
- **Integrates globally** — can coordinate across markets (factor-cost arbitrage, strategic response to rivals, coordinated pricing and product moves).

This is exactly what **internalization theory / market-imperfections approach** formalises: ownership **internalises** value that an arm's-length contract cannot efficiently secure. Theory depth is covered in [T12 — FDI Theories](T12%20-%20FDI%20Theories.md); here the point is that the three weaknesses *are* the reasons why internalization pays off.

## 6. Key terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Licensing | Contractual grant of the right to use intangible property (patents, trademarks, know-how) to a foreign licensee in exchange for a royalty; arm's-length mode of entry. |
| FDI (Foreign Direct Investment) | Equity investment in a foreign productive asset giving the investor **control** (typically ≥10% of capital). |
| Internalization / Market-imperfections approach | Theory explaining why firms prefer FDI over licensing: ownership internalises value that contracts cannot efficiently protect or coordinate. (Spelling: **internalization**, not "internationalization".) |
| Tight control | Direct managerial oversight over production, marketing, strategy, and coordination across markets — available under FDI, not under licensing. |
| Tacit / firm-specific capabilities | Management, organisational-culture, and manufacturing know-how embedded in people and routines; hard to codify, therefore not contractable. |
| Global oligopoly | Industry with a handful of interdependent multinational competitors (Coca-Cola/Pepsi, Boeing/Airbus) where strategic interdependence forces coordinated global responses. |
| Multipoint competition | Oligopolistic rivalry played out simultaneously in multiple national markets, requiring coordinated moves. |
| Factor-cost differences | Cross-country differences in wages, capital costs, land, energy, etc., that firms exploit by dispersing production — control over this dispersion is one reason to prefer FDI. |
| Figure 8.4 (Hill) | Decision tree for choosing among exporting, licensing, and FDI based on transport costs/tariffs, know-how amenability, need for control, and IP protectability. |

## 8. Worked examples

- **Coca-Cola vs. Pepsi** — canonical **global-oligopoly** example. Neither could license its foreign operations because the two firms must coordinate product launches, pricing, and marketing across markets to respond to each other. (Teacher's chosen illustration — use this on the exam.)
- **Boeing vs. Airbus** — a second global-oligopoly example in the same category; strategic interdependence demands FDI, not licensing.
- **Toyota / Japanese automakers** — illustrate weakness §2.3 (capabilities not amenable to licensing) and §2.2 (control). Cultural + strategic reason not to license.
- **Matsushita → Sony** (textbook) — illustrates weakness §2.1 (licensing creates a future competitor).
- **Pizza / McDonald's** — *counter-example*: where licensing/franchising **does** work. Know-how is codifiable, tight control is not required, the brand+operations manual travel well. Use as the contrast case on the exam.
