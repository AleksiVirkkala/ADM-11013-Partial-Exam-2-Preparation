# T03 — US Agricultural Subsidies (Mexico's complaints)

**Appears in lectures:** L16, L17
**Textbook:** Hill Ch. 7 (tariffs and other instruments of trade policy)
**Exam priority:** HIGH — teacher flagged as "certain on test" multiple times ("I'm certain to put this on the test… I think it's so important. I want to put it on the test." — L16; "I think I'll answer this on the second exam… What are those three classes of agricultural subsidies?" — L17)

## 1. TL;DR
The US supports its farmers through **three** kinds of subsidies — a **price floor** (USDA pays the gap when the market price falls below a guaranteed minimum), a **set-aside / fallow program** (farmers paid *not* to produce every ~7–8 years, ostensibly for soil conservation), and **technical assistance** (research and extension services delivered by US land-grant / agricultural-scientific universities). The combined effect is chronic US over-production and artificially depressed world prices plus a permanent productivity gap, which Mexican farmers — whose largest trading partner by far is the US and who receive no comparable support — say makes fair competition impossible.

## 2. The three subsidies (must know by name)

### 2.1 Price floor (minimum price)
- **Mechanism:** The US Department of Agriculture guarantees a minimum price for a commodity (wheat, corn, sugar, cotton, etc.). If the market price falls **below** the floor, the USDA pays the farmer the **difference**. If the market price rises **above** the floor, the farmer keeps the full upside (no clawback).
  - Worked example from class: floor = $100,000/ton; if market drops to $90,000, USDA pays $10,000/ton; if market rises to $110,000, the farmer pockets the full $110,000.
- **Effect on Mexico / world price:** Guaranteed income → US farmers keep planting → chronic **over-production** → **world price depressed**. Mexican farmers, who have **no equivalent** price-floor program, receive less for their output and cannot match the guaranteed-income position of their US counterparts.

### 2.2 Set-aside / Fallow program
- **Mechanism:** Every ~7–8 years (teacher said "every 7th, 8th or 9th year, **depending on the product**") the farmer is paid **not to produce**, leaving the land **fallow** (wild). Payment is calibrated to the farmer's normal output from that land.
- **Stated rationale:** Soil conservation — continuous cultivation of cotton, sugar, wheat or corn depletes nutrients; a fallow year lets the soil re-enrich.
- **Effect:** Also props up prices by removing output from the market. Teacher's side observation: the US Midwest *still* over-produces so much that in autumn grain is piled in the streets for lack of silo space, further depressing world prices — showing the program does not neutralise the over-supply problem, just manages it.
- **EU analogue:** The EU pays farmers to convert land into forests for conservation — similar idea (withdraw production, preserve land).

### 2.3 Technical assistance (from agricultural / scientific universities)
- **Mechanism:** The US government funds **land-grant universities and agricultural extension services**. Scientists are dispatched to farms to advise on seed varieties, crop rotation, irrigation, machinery, and production techniques.
- **Effect on comparative productivity:** Systematically raises US farm productivity. Mexico now has **some** version of this (unlike the first two subsidies), but the US does it at far greater scale and with much longer institutional history, so the productivity gap persists.

## 3. Why it matters for Mexico
- Mexico's **biggest trading partner by far is the USA** — so distortions in US farm markets flow straight into Mexican markets.
- Mexican farmers have **no equivalent** price-floor program and **no equivalent** set-aside program; the technical-assistance gap is smaller but still wide.
- The complaint is shared by other emerging markets (and lodged against US, Canadian, and EU subsidies alike), but Mexico feels it most because of NAFTA/USMCA proximity and trade volume.
- **Why did Mexico accept this in the USMCA negotiation?** Mexico chose other battles (tariffs, rules of origin). The subsidies issue is better handled through the **WTO** channel. Mexico does not like the arrangement but lives with it — a recurring irritant at NAFTA / T-MEC renegotiations.
- **Student-raised counter-examples** (not the three "main" US types): Mexico has *some* water subsidies for rural areas, some seed subsidies, and tax deductions on agricultural equipment — but nothing comparable in scale.

## 4. Key terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Price floor (minimum price)** | USDA-guaranteed minimum price for a commodity; if market price falls below, government pays the farmer the difference; if market price is higher, farmer keeps the upside. |
| **Set-aside / fallow program** | US subsidy that pays farmers *not* to produce for one year every ~7–8 (up to 9) years; stated rationale is soil conservation. |
| **Fallow** | Land deliberately left uncultivated (wild) for a season so nutrients regenerate. |
| **Technical assistance (subsidy)** | US government-funded research and extension service delivered through land-grant / agricultural-scientific universities — raises US farm productivity. |
| **Land-grant university / agricultural extension** | The US institutional vehicle for technical assistance: universities with a public mandate to research and disseminate farming knowledge to working farmers. |
| **Over-production** | Sustained output above market-clearing demand, depressing the world price — the recurring side-effect of the price floor and (partly) the set-aside program. |
| **World price depression** | Downward pressure on international commodity prices caused by subsidised surplus output being dumped on world markets. |
| **Subsidy (as non-tariff barrier)** | Government transfer to domestic producers that distorts international competition — listed in L16 as a **non-tariff barrier** alongside quotas, import licenses, etc. |
| **USMCA / T-MEC / NAFTA** | The trade agreement between Mexico, the US, and Canada; the forum at which Mexico has *not* successfully dismantled these subsidies. |
| **WTO channel** | The multilateral venue Mexico prefers for pursuing the subsidy dispute (rather than bilateral / USMCA). |

## 7. Watch out
- **Set-aside interval** wavers between **7, 8, and 9** years in the transcript. Safest phrasing if asked for a number: *"every 7–8 years (teacher also said 'up to 9 depending on the product')."* Do **not** commit to a single integer.
- **Price floor is asymmetric** — pays the gap when the market is low, but does **not** claw back when the market is high. This asymmetry is the reason it causes over-production rather than just stabilising income; worth stating explicitly if the exam asks *why* the floor distorts the world price.
- **Technical assistance is the one Mexico partially matches** — don't claim Mexico has "no" equivalent; say Mexico has *some* version, but far less than the US.
- **Classify subsidies as non-tariff barriers**, not tariff barriers — an easy trap question.
- **Don't confuse with EU programs** — the EU pays farmers to **convert land to forest** (conservation framing), which is analogous but not identical to the US set-aside.
- The subsidy complaint is a **Mexican position**, but also shared by other emerging markets against US, **Canadian**, and **EU** subsidies. If the exam asks "who complains?", say "emerging markets, Mexico foremost among them because the US is its largest trading partner."
