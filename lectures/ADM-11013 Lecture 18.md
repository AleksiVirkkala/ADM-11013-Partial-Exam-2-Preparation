## 3. Core concepts

### 3.1 The 8 steps of an export–import operation
1. **Negotiation between buyer and exporter** over the contract, framed around the 4 Ps (product, price, place, promotion/publicity). Finding clients: foreign-trade bank fairs (e.g. **Bancomext** in Mexico), trade fairs, catalogues, internet advertising.
2. **Exporting party chooses a customs agent** (external customs broker) or uses an internal in-house employee to handle the transaction.
3. **Customs broker prepares the Pedimento** (import/export tax declaration) and reviews the details with the client.
4. **Exporter prepares the Certificate of Origin, Packing List, and Invoice** and sends copies to the exporter's and importer's customs agents.
5. **Bill of Lading** is issued (the 5th document) with its three functions.
6. **Merchandise is shipped to customs of the importing country.**
7. **Customs agent pays duties and taxes** to customs; Pedimento is stamped as proof of payment.
8. **Importer receives the merchandise** and brings the goods into the country.

### 3.2 Customs agent vs. customs broker vs. customs (review)
- **Customs agent / customs broker** — private-sector business; a consultant that handles import/export documentation and moves product through customs. In Mexico, "Agencia Aduanal"; many are clustered around Mexico City airport Terminal 1 with warehouses.
- **Customs** — the government authority.
- If you have no external broker, an **in-house employee** can perform the role.

### 3.3 The 5 essential documents

| # | Document | Prepared by | Purpose |
|---|----------|-------------|---------|
| 1 | **Pedimento** | Customs Broker (or in-house employee) | Mexican government import/export tax declaration; contains the **Harmonized Customs Code** (8 digits) used to compute the tariff. It is stamped on payment and without it the goods cannot leave customs. |
| 2 | **Certificate of Origin** | Exporter | Certifies where the goods come from; based on the **Rules of Origin** (two formulas covered earlier). |
| 3 | **Packing List** | Exporter | Itemises what is physically in each box/package. |
| 4 | **Invoice** | Exporter / Seller | Commercial invoice — standard sales document. |
| 5 | **Bill of Lading** | Shipping/transport company (issued to the exporter) | Has three functions (see 3.4). |

### 3.4 Bill of Lading — 3 functions (must memorise)
1. **Contract** between the shipper (exporter) and the transport/shipping company.
2. **Document of legal possession / right to possess the goods** — analogous to a car title; proves you are not a pirate on the high seas.
3. **Delivery receipt (proof of delivery)** — the importer must present a copy to the transport company to take possession of the goods.

Alternative transport variant: **Airway Bill** (for air freight) — same legal function. The example shown was a Cathay Pacific Airways airway bill from Hong Kong (Wish International Logistics Ltd.) to Mexico City.

### 3.5 The Pedimento in detail (from the Primex Laguna / Siemens example)
The teacher worked through an actual 2022 Mexican import Pedimento. Key fields:
- **Agencia Aduanal** — name of customs broker (e.g. Primex Laguna S.C.; Arturo / Alejandro Sánchez e Hijos S.C.).
- **Aduana de entrada/salida** — customs point of entry (example: Nuevo Laredo, Tamaulipas — across from Laredo, Texas on the Rio Grande).
- **Tipo de operación** — IMP (import) or EXP (export); **Régimen**: A1 = definitive import (not temporary — the goods are not returning to the country of origin).
- **Tipo de cambio** — USD→MXN exchange rate (≈20.66 at May 2022).
- **Medio de transporte** — mode of arrival/exit (code 7 ≈ by truck / highway).
- **Peso bruto** — gross weight in kg.
- **Fracción arancelaria** — the 8-digit Harmonized Customs Code (example `83021099` = metal fittings/mountings, consistent with *bisagras* = hinges).
- **Value fields** — value in USD, value in MXN at customs, commercial value (excl. freight), freight, insurance, "otros incrementables".
- **Tax fields**:
  - **IGI** (Impuesto General de Importación) = **duties** → **0%** under T-MEC.
  - **PRV** (*prevalidación*) — small pre-validation fee.
  - **IVA** (*Impuesto al Valor Agregado*) = **16% VAT** — still charged even when IGI = 0.
  - **DTA** (derecho de trámite aduanero) — a "right" rather than a tax (not deductible), but functionally a fee.
- **Liquidación** — total amount owed; paid at a commercial bank branch next to customs (example: BBVA). Without the stamped proof of payment, goods cannot be released.
- **Datos del proveedor** — supplier's name, address, invoice number + date; must match the attached invoice. A mismatch is a problem.
- **Incoterm** — 3-letter code (DAP, CPT…) specifying who pays what.
- A Pedimento can **consolidate shipments from multiple suppliers** (example: Siemens Mobility – Tualatin OR; Superior Metal Products – Sugar Grove IL; Wilson Tool – White Bear Lake MN, all consolidated in Laredo TX before crossing).

### 3.6 Incoterms (introduction only)
- 11 Incoterms total, used worldwide, 3-letter codes.
- Define **who pays** for transport, insurance, duties/taxes, and at **what point** risk and cost transfer between exporter and importer.
- Examples seen in the Pedimento:
  - **DAP** — *Delivered At Place* (also written "delivered at port/place").
  - **CPT** — *Carriage Paid To*.
- Formal name: *International Commercial Terms of Trade* (ICC). Full treatment deferred — likely Lecture 19.

### 3.7 Duties under T-MEC (review from Lecture 17)
- Categories of tariff removal: **A, B, C, C+**.
  - A = removed immediately.
  - B = over 5 years (MX) / equivalents in CA & US.
  - C = 10 years (MX), 15 years (US).
- A good **does not have to be in the same category in all three countries** — each country negotiated its own schedule. ("True or false: if A in one country, A in all three? False.")
- Net effect on the example Pedimento: IGI = 0; VAT still applies.

### 3.8 Rules of Origin (review)
- Two formulas (covered earlier in the course) used to determine where a good "comes from" for preferential-tariff purposes.
- Certificate of Origin is prepared by the exporter based on these rules.
- Example subtlety: some metal parts made in Mexico are shipped to the US, incorporated into another good, and re-exported to Mexico ("a lot of the pieces come back and forth across the border").

### 3.9 The 4 Ps (for the export-plan assignment)
Standard marketing 4 Ps, as used in the export contract / plan:
1. **Product** — what is being sold (incl. compliance with foreign norms).
2. **Price** — selling price, taking duties, VAT, freight, and exchange rate into account.
3. **Place / Physical distribution / Logistics** — the 4th P in marketing is sometimes "Physical distribution"; the teacher equates it with logistics.
4. **Promotion / Publicity** — promotion, advertising, channel strategy.

Think about: 8-digit customs code, duties abroad, insurance, export credits, why Mexican wine struggles to export while Chilean, Argentine, Australian, European, and Californian wines dominate.

## 4. Definitions / terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Pedimento** | Mexican government import/export tax declaration form, prepared by the customs broker. |
| **Customs broker / customs agent (Agencia Aduanal)** | Private-sector consultancy that handles import/export documentation and moves goods through customs. |
| **Harmonized Customs Code (HCC) / Fracción arancelaria** | 8-digit product classification code, identical worldwide, used to determine the tariff. |
| **Certificate of Origin** | Exporter-issued document certifying the country of origin under the Rules of Origin. |
| **Rules of Origin** | Two formulas (covered earlier) determining where a product "comes from" for tariff purposes. |
| **Packing List** | Exporter-issued list of the contents of each package. |
| **Invoice (Factura)** | Commercial invoice issued by the exporter/seller. |
| **Bill of Lading (Conocimiento de embarque)** | Shipping document with three functions: contract, proof of right to possession, and delivery receipt. |
| **Airway Bill** | Air-freight equivalent of the Bill of Lading; same legal functions. |
| **Incoterms** | 11 three-letter International Commercial Terms of Trade defining who pays transport, insurance, duties, and at what point responsibility transfers. |
| **DAP** | Delivered At Place (sometimes "at port"). |
| **CPT** | Carriage Paid To. |
| **IGI** | *Impuesto General de Importación* — import duty (= 0% under T-MEC). |
| **IVA** | *Impuesto al Valor Agregado* — VAT, 16% in Mexico; still charged under T-MEC. |
| **DTA** | *Derecho de Trámite Aduanero* — a customs-processing "right" (fee). Not deductible. |
| **PRV / Prevalidación** | Pre-validation fee on the Pedimento. |
| **Régimen A1** | Definitive import regime (not temporary; goods will not return). |
| **Aduana** | Customs (Spanish). |
| **RFC** | Mexican tax identification number (*Registro Federal de Contribuyentes*). |
| **CURP** | Mexican personal population/identification number. |
| **Bancomext** | Mexican foreign trade bank; organises fairs to connect Mexican exporters with foreign buyers. |
| **Bisagras** | Hinges (the example export product). |
| **Letter of Credit** | Banking instrument used to secure international-trade payment; deferred to a later lecture. |
| **4 Ps** | Product, Price, Place (physical distribution / logistics), Promotion (publicity) — marketing/export-plan framework. |
