## 3. Core concepts

### 3.1 Transfer pricing strategy — the idea
A **multinational corporation (MNC)** sets **internal (intra-company) prices** between its own subsidiaries in different countries so that profit appears in the **low-tax country** and cost/expense appears in the **high-tax country**. Result: lower combined income tax, higher group net profit.

- In practice firms **do not do it flagrantly**, because a zero-profit in a high-tax country would attract an audit. They aim for a **grey area** — plausible commercial pricing that nudges profit toward the low-tax jurisdiction.
- No harmonized global tax system exists. Proposals (e.g., Biden's push for a global minimum corporate tax) have not been approved at scale. Within the EU, Germany and others dislike Ireland's 12.5% rate but cannot force harmonization — the EU is a monetary/trade union, **not a fiscal union** (a source of tension since the 2008/2011 crises, e.g., Germany vs. Greece).

### 3.2 Two-country scenario (see §5 for the numbers)
- Country A: lower tax (40%). Country B: higher tax (50%).
- **Without** the strategy: A sells to B at a normal price; profit arises in both countries and both are taxed.
- **With** the strategy: A **raises** the intra-company selling price to B, squeezing B's margin to zero. All profit is recognized in A, taxed only at 40%. Combined net profit rises.

### 3.3 Three-country tax-haven chain
- Country A (e.g., US, 40%) → Country B (e.g., **Bermuda**, 0% — paper/shell "pass-through" entity) → Country C (e.g., UK, 50%).
- A sells to B at cost (no profit in A). B resells to C at a high price (profit parks in B, taxed at 0%). C sells to the public at cost (no profit in C).
- **All profit is domiciled in the tax haven.** Bermuda benefits modestly through employment, personal income tax, and fees (not corporate tax).
- If the manipulation is too extreme it becomes "flagrant" and triggers audits. Firms therefore prefer a **gray area**: trimming the high-tax profit by 5–10%, not to zero.

### 3.4 The four mechanisms MNCs use to reduce tax liability (Hill Ch. 20) — **EXAM**

#### (1) Charging dividends
- Structure a subsidiary in a low-tax country as the **parent/owner** of the subsidiary in the high-tax country.
- Example: **Microsoft Ireland owns Microsoft Mexico.** Mexico pays dividends up to Ireland on its profits.
- Effect: **expense/cash outflow in Mexico** (dividends up), **income in Ireland**, taxed at only 12.5%.

#### (2) Charging royalties on intellectual property
- Place the patents, brands, and copyrights in the **low-tax subsidiary** (e.g., Microsoft Ireland).
- The high-tax subsidiary (Microsoft Mexico) must pay royalties to use those patents/brands/copyrights when it sells products.
- Effect: **expenses go up in Mexico**, **income goes up in Ireland** (taxed at the lower Irish rate).

#### (3) Transfer pricing
- Artificially **raise or reduce** the intra-company price of goods or services between sister subsidiaries in order to locate profit in the low-tax country.
- Not based on market pricing; based on tax objectives.
- See §5 for the worked example.

#### (4) Fronting loans
- A **disguised intra-group loan routed through an unrelated bank** so the tax authorities do not see a direct parent-subsidiary financing relationship.
- Structure (text diagram):

  ```
  Subsidiary in TAX HAVEN          Intermediary BANK          Subsidiary in HIGH-TAX country
  (e.g., Bermuda, 0%)   ──────►   (e.g., "Barclays")  ──────►  (e.g., UK, 50%)
         deposits $50M                                            receives loan $50M
         earns 9% interest  ◄──── pays 9% ◄─── pays 9–10% ◄────   pays interest on loan
  ```

- The Bermuda subsidiary (cash-rich, taxed at 0%) deposits, say, **$50 million** at a bank and earns **9%** interest tax-free.
- The bank **lends the same money to the UK subsidiary**, which pays **9–10%** interest to the bank.
- UK side: interest is a **deductible expense** → lowers UK taxable income.
- Bermuda side: interest is **income taxed at 0%**.
- **Why the bank in the middle?** A direct loan between the two subsidiaries would alert tax authorities. The bank obscures the connection — the firms want to make the structure **deliberately complex** to pass audits. ("It will [at]tract the tax authorities" if done directly.)

### 3.5 Who is doing this, in practice
- Apple, Nike, Uber, Starbucks, Amazon (teacher's named examples).
- **Uber Mexico:** invoices carry an Amsterdam address — Mexican revenue is channelled to the Netherlands subsidiary.
- **Apple / "Double Irish Dutch Sandwich"**: Apple's Irish subsidiary received payments from Apple subsidiaries across Europe, Middle East, and outside the US, to avoid US income tax. In 2020 Apple accumulated **$252B in cash in Ireland** (country population ~5–5.5M). Apple further routed Irish money through a **Jersey (Channel Island, 0% tax)** subsidiary that charged royalties and dividends to Apple subsidiaries worldwide. The joke "double Irish" = a double shot of Irish whiskey.

### 3.6 Principal tax havens (to recognize / list)
Ireland (12.5%), Netherlands (25% but with favourable structures), Luxembourg, Switzerland (secrecy laws), Bahamas, Cayman Islands, British Virgin Islands, Bermuda, Panama, Belize, Hong Kong (16.5%), Singapore, Channel Islands (Jersey, Guernsey, Sark — 0%).

Reference marginal **corporate** tax rates mentioned:
- Mexico **30%**, USA federal **21%** (plus state, e.g., California +13% → ~34% effective in CA), Ireland **12.5%**, Cayman/Bahamas/Jersey **0%**, Netherlands **25%**, Hong Kong **16.5%**, France **31%**, Germany **29.65%**.
- Note: under Trump in **2018** the US federal corporate rate dropped from **35% to 21%** — this made the US more competitive relative to Mexico and was seen by some Mexican economists (e.g., Luis Pazos) as a threat that could pull investment out of Mexico.

## 4. Definitions / terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| MNC | Multinational corporation — a firm operating subsidiaries in multiple countries. |
| Transfer pricing | The internal price charged between sister subsidiaries of the same MNC; abused to locate profit in low-tax jurisdictions. |
| Transfer pricing strategy | Deliberate raising/lowering of intra-company prices to shift profit from high-tax to low-tax countries. |
| Tax haven | A jurisdiction with very low or zero corporate income tax (and often secrecy) used to domicile profits. |
| Shell corporation / paper entity | A subsidiary that has no real factory or customers in the host country — only an office and invoices — used as a pass-through. |
| Pass-through entity | An intermediate subsidiary through which sales are invoiced to shift profit. |
| Fronting loan | An intra-group loan routed through a third-party (unrelated) bank to disguise the parent-subsidiary financing link and shift interest expense/income between tax jurisdictions. |
| Charging dividends (mechanism 1) | Low-tax parent subsidiary owns high-tax subsidiary; the latter pays dividends up, shifting profit. |
| Charging royalties (mechanism 2) | Placing IP (patents, brands, copyrights) in the low-tax subsidiary, which then charges royalties to the high-tax subsidiaries. |
| Double Irish (Dutch Sandwich) | An Apple-era structure routing profits through Ireland and then on to a zero-tax channel-island subsidiary. |
| Pedimento | Mexican customs declaration — one of the 5 export documents. |
| Bill of lading | Transport document (carrier contract, receipt, document of title) — has **3 purposes** (exam-relevant). |
| Incoterms | International Commercial Terms (ICC); organized in **4 groups**. |
| SAT | *Servicio de Administración Tributaria* — Mexico's federal tax authority. |
| Fiscal union | A union with harmonized taxation (which the EU is **not**). |

## 5. Worked examples / exercises

### 5.1 Two-country transfer pricing (numbers from the handout)

Country A marginal income tax = **40%**, Country B = **50%**. One company owns both subsidiaries. Country A produces and sells only to B; B sells to the public.

**Scenario 1 — without transfer-pricing strategy**

| Item | Country A | Country B |
|---|---:|---:|
| Sales | $10,000 (to B) | $12,000 (to public) |
| Cost of sales | $8,000 | $10,000 |
| Profit (pre-tax) | $2,000 | $2,000 |
| Income tax | $800 (40%) | $1,000 (50%) |
| Net profit | $1,200 | $1,000 |

**Combined net profit = $2,200.**

**Scenario 2 — with transfer-pricing strategy** (A raises its intra-company price to B from $10,000 → $12,000)

| Item | Country A | Country B |
|---|---:|---:|
| Sales | $12,000 (to B) | $12,000 (to public) |
| Cost of sales | $8,000 | $12,000 |
| Profit (pre-tax) | $4,000 | $0 |
| Income tax | $1,600 (40%) | $0 (50%) |
| Net profit | $2,400 | $0 |

**Combined net profit = $2,400. Saving = $200** (illustrative; real numbers are in millions and not this flagrant).

> Teacher's warning: zero profit in the high-tax country is "very flagrant" and will attract an audit. In practice firms trim the high-tax profit only partially.

### 5.2 Three-country tax-haven chain (US → Bermuda → UK)

Country A = USA (40%), Country B = **Bermuda (0%, paper entity)**, Country C = UK (50%).

| Item | US | Bermuda (paper) | UK |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Sales | $8,000 (to Bermuda) | $12,000 (to UK) | $12,000 (to public) |
| Cost of sales | $8,000 | $8,000 | $12,000 |
| Profit | $0 | $4,000 | $0 |
| Income tax | $0 | $0 | $0 |
| Net profit | $0 | $4,000 | $0 |

All profit ($4,000) is parked in Bermuda, taxed at 0%. Bermuda has no factory and no local customers; it exists only as an invoice-routing "paper" subsidiary.

### 5.3 Fronting-loan numerical example
- UK subsidiary (50% tax, "high-tax") needs financing. Bermuda subsidiary (0% tax, "cash-rich haven") has funds.
- Direct loan from Bermuda → UK would attract tax authorities.
- Instead: Bermuda deposits **$50 million** at an intermediary bank (e.g., Barclays) earning **9% interest**.
- The bank lends $50M to the UK subsidiary. The UK subsidiary pays **9–10% interest** to the bank.
- Flows:
  - UK: interest expense ↑ → taxable income ↓ → 50% tax on a smaller base.
  - Bermuda: interest income ↑ → taxed at 0%.
  - Bank: pockets a thin spread, provides the legal distance.
- Net effect: group has moved profit from the UK to Bermuda via the appearance of a commercial bank loan.
