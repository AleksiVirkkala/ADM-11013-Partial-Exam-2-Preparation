## 3. Core concepts

### 3.1 Limitations of exporting (*Book: p. 229 / p. 238 online*)
1. **Transport costs** — when added to production costs, exporting becomes unprofitable for products with a **low value-to-weight ratio** that can be produced almost anywhere.
   - *Example:* **CEMEX** (Mexican cement producer) expanded via FDI rather than export because cement is heavy and cheap per ton; CEMEX now has plants across Europe, Indonesia, the US and Latin America.
   - For high value-to-weight goods (electronics, pharma), transport is a minor cost and exporting/licensing/FDI are all viable.
2. **Trade barriers (actual or threatened)** — tariffs and quotas push firms toward FDI to "jump" the barrier.
   - *Example:* Japanese automakers in the **1980s** feared a US backlash against their exports and set up US plants (and later Mexican plants) in response.

### 3.2 Limitations of licensing (*Book: p. 238*)
Three core weaknesses that explain why firms prefer FDI:

1. **Giving away know-how** — licensing can hand valuable technological know-how to a potential foreign competitor who may later break the contract and compete directly.
2. **Loss of tight control** — the licensor cannot tightly control production, marketing, and strategy in the foreign market, which may be necessary to maximize profitability. (Japanese firms famously avoid licensing for this reason — partly cultural, partly strategic.)
3. **Capabilities not amenable to licensing** — when a firm's competitive advantage lies not in the product itself but in **management, marketing, and manufacturing capabilities**, these intangibles cannot be packaged into a licensing contract.

**Summary rule (p. 238 last paragraph):** Licensing is inadequate when (a) valuable know-how cannot be adequately protected by a licensing contract, (b) tight control over the foreign entity is needed to maximize market share, or (c) the firm's skills/know-how are not amenable to licensing.

### 3.3 When licensing is NOT a good option (3 industry types) (*Book: p. 244 / p. 253, "Focus on Managerial Implications"*)
1. **High-technology industries** where protecting firm-specific expertise is paramount (licensing is hazardous — leaks IP).
2. **Global oligopolies** with competitive interdependence, where MNEs must maintain tight control over foreign operations to launch coordinated attacks against global rivals.
   - Examples: **Coca-Cola vs. Pepsi**, **Boeing vs. Airbus**.
3. **Industries under intense cost pressure** where MNEs must keep tight control to disperse production to the most favorable factor-cost locations, minimizing cost and maximizing value.

### 3.4 Theories of FDI vs. Licensing (the three theories)

#### (a) Internalization theory
- Answers: *why own rather than contract out?*
- The firm **internalizes** the value of ownership instead of relying on a **contractual relationship** (licensing) with a third party.
- Motivated by the three limitations of licensing above — protecting know-how, keeping tight control, and exploiting non-codifiable capabilities.

#### (b) Strategic behavior theory (Knickerbocker — implicit in the textbook)
- FDI is an **imitative / copycat move** within an **oligopoly**: when one major rival invests abroad, competitors follow to maintain their strategic position (multipoint competition).
- *Example:* **Boeing** opened a plant in **South Carolina (US)**; shortly after, **Airbus** opened a plant in **Alabama** (lower wages and land costs). Boeing also has operations in **Sheffield, UK**, arguably near Airbus' European footprint.
- Key idea: firms follow each other to **not lose strategic advantage**.

#### (c) Eclectic Paradigm — John Dunning (*Book: ~p. 240*)
- The third theory. Dunning accepts the internalization argument (it's hard to license unique capabilities) and adds **location-specific advantages**: advantages that arise from using resource endowments or assets **tied to a particular foreign location** that the firm wants to combine with its own unique assets.
- Combining those location-specific assets with the firm's unique capabilities often *requires* FDI, because the firm must establish production where those assets are.
- **Externalities / spillovers** — knowledge spills over within clusters; well-established theory says firms benefit by locating close to the source.
- **Canonical cluster examples:**
  - **Silicon Valley (USA)** — Stanford + UC Berkeley produce a dense supply of PhDs and technical talent; labour flows freely between neighbouring firms (Apple, Google, Oracle, HP, etc.).
  - **Automobile industry** — clusters geographically (e.g. Baden-Württemberg and Bavaria in southern Germany).
  - **Petroleum / energy** — US and Mexico.
  - **Banking** — Switzerland (Zurich, Geneva).
  - **Distribution / logistics** — the Netherlands (port of Rotterdam + highway network).
  - **Agriculture / commodities** — Chicago, historically.
  - **Technology in Mexico** — Guadalajara area (e.g. Apple operations).
  - **Fertilizer production** — European Russia (west of the Urals).

**Exam-answer template for the Eclectic Paradigm:** *Firms seek to take advantage of **location-specific advantages** by locating where **externalities** (spillovers of human capital, infrastructure, and know-how) are concentrated. They establish a **nucleus/cluster** where resources are abundant, and this typically requires FDI because the unique capabilities cannot be licensed.*

### 3.5 Figure 8.4 — Decision framework for mode of entry
A cascading decision tree:

1. **How high are transportation costs & tariffs?**
   - Low → **Export**.
   - High → continue.
2. **Is know-how amenable to licensing?**
   - No → **FDI**.
   - Yes → continue.
3. **Is tight control over foreign operations required?**
   - Yes → **FDI**.
   - No → continue.
4. **Can know-how be adequately protected by a licensing contract?**
   - No → **FDI**.
   - Yes → **License**.

*Classic licensing/franchising success case:* pizza / restaurant chains (e.g. McDonald's), where know-how is easily codified and the brand+operations manual travel well.

## 4. Definitions / terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **FDI (Foreign Direct Investment)** | Investment by a firm in productive assets in a foreign country, typically giving control. |
| **Portfolio investment** | Passive financial investment (shares, bonds) without managerial control — usually by individuals. |
| **Maquiladora** | Mexican manufacturing-under-license / contract-manufacturing model; key to Mexico's economic opening. |
| **Turnkey project** | Contractor designs, builds, and delivers a ready-to-operate facility to a foreign client. |
| **Internalization** | Bringing a transaction inside the firm (ownership) instead of using an arm's-length contract (licensing); the value of ownership vs. contract. |
| **Strategic behavior (FDI)** | FDI as an **imitative** move by oligopolists to match rivals' foreign moves and preserve competitive parity. |
| **Eclectic Paradigm (OLI)** | Dunning's framework: FDI occurs when a firm has Ownership advantages + Location-specific advantages + Internalization advantages. In this lecture, the emphasis was on **location-specific advantages**. |
| **Location-specific advantage** | Advantage arising from resource endowments or assets tied to a particular foreign location that the firm combines with its own unique assets. |
| **Externalities / spillovers** | Knowledge and skill leakage within a geographic cluster; firms benefit by locating near the source. |
| **Cluster / nucleus** | Geographic concentration of firms in the same industry drawing on shared location-specific advantages. |
| **Global oligopoly** | Industry with a handful of interdependent global players (e.g. Coca-Cola/Pepsi, Boeing/Airbus). |
| **Value-to-weight ratio** | Product's unit value divided by unit weight; low ratios make exporting uneconomic. |
| **Home country / Host country** | Home = where the investing firm is based; Host = where the investment is received. |
