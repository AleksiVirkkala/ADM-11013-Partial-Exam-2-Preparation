## 1. Overview
This lecture is the international-trade mechanics unit. It covers how cross-border transactions are actually executed: the letter of credit (LC) as a bank-guaranteed payment vehicle between parties who don't trust each other, the Incoterms that allocate cost/risk along the shipping chain, and factoring as a cash-flow tool. It also drills the five core export documents and the 8-digit harmonized code, and closes with a wine export-plan exercise using the 4Ps.

## 3. Core concepts

### 3.1 The 5 required export documents
| # | Document | Role |
|---|----------|------|
| 1 | Invoice | Commercial/tax record of the sale |
| 2 | Bill of Lading | Ownership + transport contract + delivery receipt |
| 3 | Pedimento | Mexican customs/tax document |
| 4 | Certificate of Origin | Proves eligibility for free-trade-agreement benefits |
| 5 | Packing List | Details of what's physically in the shipment |

Additional documents may be required for specific products — e.g., sanitary/phytosanitary certificates for fruit and other food shipments.

### 3.2 Harmonized System (HS) code
The **8-digit HS code** is the element that unambiguously identifies what a good is (e.g., "this is a laptop"). It is **the same in every country** — this universality is what makes international classification and tariff lookup possible. Note however that a good's **tariff removal category** (A, B, C…) under an FTA can differ between partner countries because those categories are separately negotiated.

### 3.3 Letter of Credit (LC)
**Purpose.** An LC is used when buyer and seller don't know or trust each other but want to do business (e.g., a Mexican machinery exporter selling to a buyer in Romania). Neither party will front the goods or the payment without a guarantee. A bank (for a fee) steps in between, transforming a bilateral-trust problem into a bank-backed promise.

**Definition.** An LC is an **irrevocable promise of payment by the issuing bank to the seller, through the advising bank**, based on the sale contract. The issuing bank will pay only once **compliant documents** are presented within a specified time.

**Four parties in an (unconfirmed documentary) LC**:
1. **Seller** (beneficiary) — receives the payment.
2. **Buyer** (applicant) — applies for the LC; obligated to pay.
3. **Issuing bank** — the buyer's bank; issues the LC and is obligated to pay the seller.
4. **Advising bank** — the seller's bank; no obligation to pay, but authenticates the LC and handles documents. (If no direct correspondent relationship exists, a global bank like Citibank or Deutsche Bank is often used as a correspondent.)

**Stage 1 — Opening the LC**
1. Buyer and seller sign a **sale contract** that specifies payment via LC.
2. Buyer **applies** to the issuing bank for the LC; the bank runs compliance/solvency checks on the buyer (effectively extending credit) and sets terms/conditions.
3. Issuing bank issues the LC and sends it to the **advising bank**.
4. Advising bank performs its own checks and **forwards the LC to the seller**.
5. Seller **reviews terms and conditions** and requests any necessary amendments through the issuing bank. (This is the seller's responsibility — any discrepancy later risks losing the payment guarantee.)

**Stage 2 — Using the LC**
6. Seller **ships the goods** and compiles the documents required by the LC; presents those LC documents to the advising bank, which checks compliance.
7. Advising bank **forwards the documents** to the issuing bank for settlement; issuing bank re-checks compliance.
8. If compliant, the issuing bank **arranges settlement** with the advising bank and **releases the LC documents to the buyer**. The buyer *must* receive these documents — crucially the Bill of Lading — to take possession of the goods and clear customs. The issuing bank only releases them once the buyer has either paid or obtained a loan from the issuing bank.
9. Advising bank **credits the seller**. Process ends.

**Key mechanism / why it works**: the Bill of Lading ties physical possession of the goods to documentary compliance. The carrier will not release the cargo to the buyer without the buyer's copy of the Bill of Lading, and the buyer only gets it via the issuing bank after payment or credit arrangement. This is the linchpin that makes the LC enforceable.

**When to use**: especially common for trade with partners in distant or unfamiliar markets (Asia, Africa, Eastern Europe, Australia) and very common in Europe. Rare in purely domestic trade.

### 3.4 Incoterms (International Commercial Terms)
Three-letter codes (e.g., FOB, CIF, DAP, DDP) issued by the **International Chamber of Commerce (Paris)**, originating in the 1930s and updated every ~10 years. Current version: **Incoterms 2020**. They answer four practical questions for any international sale:
1. **Who arranges the freight?**
2. **Who handles export/import clearance?**
3. **Who pays import duties?**
4. **When does risk transfer from seller to buyer?**

There are 11 Incoterms; they fall into **four groups**, ordered from least seller responsibility to most:

| Group | Logic | Example(s) | Where risk transfers |
|-------|-------|------------|----------------------|
| **E** | Buyer does everything. Seller only makes goods available at own premises. | **EXW** (Ex Works) | At the seller's premises |
| **F** | Seller delivers to a carrier at the point of departure in the seller's country; buyer takes over for main carriage. | **FOB** (Free On Board) — marine shipping | After vessel loading / once goods cross the ship's rail at the port of departure |
| **C** | Seller **pays** main carriage (and sometimes insurance) to the named destination port, but **risk still transfers at origin**. | **CFR** (Cost & Freight, no insurance); **CIF** (Cost, Insurance & Freight — minimum 110%-of-invoice coverage) | On board the vessel at the origin port — not at destination! |
| **D** | Seller bears cost and risk all the way to the agreed destination point in the buyer's country. | **DAP** (Delivered At Place — buyer clears import); **DDP** (Delivered Duty Paid — seller clears import + pays duties) | At the named destination |

**Critical nuances:**
- **Group C is the "trap"**: even though the *seller pays* freight to the destination port, *risk* has already transferred at origin. Under CFR, if the ship sinks mid-voyage the buyer bears the loss — so the buyer should arrange additional insurance. CIF adds only minimum cover (110% of invoice, standard risks); smart buyers often layer on broader insurance.
- **"FOB Laredo, Texas"** is a common Americas-region misuse — FOB is officially a marine term. Never accept an invoice with just "FOB" and no named place; always insist on precise location (port, terminal).
- **EXW** is the buyer's worst case (brutal for small buyers — seller doesn't even load the truck); used when the seller has no international-logistics capability or is very dominant in the deal.
- **DDP** is the seller's worst case — seller must navigate foreign taxes, customs, and filings. Use only when the seller has trusted agents or a subsidiary in the buyer's country.
- Incoterms exist primarily to **reduce legal disputes** and provide a shared cross-jurisdictional vocabulary.

### 3.5 Factoring
**Definition**: selling your **accounts receivable** to a third party (the "factor" — typically a division of a large bank) at a discount, in exchange for immediate cash.

**Why it matters**: the #1 cause of business failure is **cash-flow shortage**. If your receivables are due in 60–90 days but you need to pay suppliers and employees *now*, factoring bridges the gap.

**Economics**: typical deal — the factor advances ~90–94% of face value in cash today and later collects the full 100% from the debtor. On a $100,000 receivable, you get $94,000 today; the factor keeps the ~$6,000 spread as its fee.

**Origin**: factoring dates back to Paris (French: *facteur*). Not a mathematical factor.

## 4. Definitions / terminology
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Letter of Credit (LC) | Irrevocable promise of payment by the issuing (buyer's) bank to the seller, through the advising (seller's) bank, conditional on presentation of compliant documents within a specified time. |
| Issuing bank | Buyer's bank; issues the LC and is legally obligated to pay on compliant documents. |
| Advising bank | Seller's bank; authenticates the LC and handles documents but has no payment obligation itself. |
| Bill of Lading | Core transport document serving three functions: proof of ownership / right to possess, contract between the carrier and the exporter, and receipt of delivery. |
| Pedimento | Mexican customs/tax declaration document. |
| Certificate of Origin | Document certifying the product's origin, enabling free-trade-agreement tariff preferences. |
| Packing List | Itemised description of the contents of the shipment. |
| 8-digit HS code | Harmonized System classification code identifying the product; identical across all countries. |
| Incoterms | Standardised 3-letter trade terms (ICC, Paris; current version 2020) specifying cost, risk, and responsibility allocation between buyer and seller; always followed by a named place. |
| EXW (Ex Works) | Group E. Seller makes goods available at own premises; buyer does everything else. |
| FOB (Free On Board) | Group F, marine. Seller clears export and loads on vessel; risk passes when goods cross the ship's rail at origin. |
| CFR (Cost & Freight) | Group C, marine. Seller pays freight to destination port; risk transfers at origin; **no insurance** included. |
| CIF (Cost, Insurance & Freight) | Group C, marine. Like CFR but seller provides minimum insurance (110% of invoice, standard risks). |
| DAP (Delivered At Place) | Group D. Seller delivers to named destination; buyer handles import customs, duties, taxes. |
| DDP (Delivered Duty Paid) | Group D. Seller handles *everything*, including import duties and taxes — maximum seller responsibility. |
| Factoring | Sale of receivables to a third party (factor) at a discount for immediate cash; cash-flow management tool. |
| Act of God | Insurance term for uncontrollable natural events; insurers frequently invoke to deny claims. |

## 5. Worked examples / exercises

### Wine export plan — Baja California producer
**Scenario**: You are the director of export for a small Baja California winery. Build an export plan using the 4Ps and ensure the 5 required trade documents are in place.

#### Product
Define precisely what is being exported:
- Type of wine (varietal, vintage, style)
- Bottle size and packaging format (case count, pallet config)
- Quality level / market positioning
- Origin/appellation of the product
- Required labelling (destination country rules on alcohol content, allergens, health warnings, language)
- **8-digit harmonized customs code** for the wine category
- Special sanitary / regulatory compliance for alcoholic beverages in the destination country

#### Promotion
Explain how you will find foreign buyers and build trust with them:
- Identify target customer or distributor type (importer, retailer, HoReCa)
- Promotion channels abroad: trade fairs, tastings, importer networks, online presence
- Whether the first transactions should use a **letter of credit** (recommended when buyer and seller don't know each other)
- Who pays at what stage, and the payment terms offered
- Trust-building mechanisms that reduce counterparty risk

#### Price
Build the export price and spell out what responsibilities it includes:
- Chosen **Incoterm** (e.g., FOB Ensenada vs. CIF Rotterdam vs. DAP buyer warehouse) — this is the single biggest driver of who pays for what
- Whether the quoted price covers transport, insurance, customs costs, or product only
- Payment conditions (prepayment, LC, open account, etc.)
- Currency (USD, EUR, MXN)
- Margin targets
- How the Incoterm allocates risk and cost between buyer and seller

#### Physical distribution
How the wine physically and financially reaches the foreign buyer:
- Delivery method and transport mode (ocean container most common for bulk wine)
- Freight forwarder / customs broker selection
- **Bank involvement** (issuing + advising banks for the LC)
- Shipping route and transit times
- Warehousing / temperature control if needed (wine is sensitive to heat)
- Handover point and procedure
- Transport document (**Bill of Lading**) — required for buyer to claim the goods

#### The 5 documents checklist (plus one)
1. Invoice
2. Bill of Lading
3. Pedimento (Mexican export customs)
4. Certificate of Origin (to claim FTA tariff preferences in the destination market)
5. Packing List
6. **Product-specific**: sanitary / alcohol-regulatory certificates required for wine in the destination country

#### Additional considerations flagged in class
- Winery capacity (can you actually supply the committed volume?)
- Competitive landscape in the target market
- Whether the first deal should be structured as LC-backed to de-risk a new relationship

## 6. Cross-references
- **Letter of credit ↔ Bill of Lading**: the LC mechanism only works because the Bill of Lading is both a title document and a delivery receipt — the buyer needs the issuing bank to release it before the carrier will hand over the goods.
- **Incoterms ↔ Price (4Ps)**: the Incoterm chosen is effectively a price-formation decision, not just a logistics one; it determines what costs are inside the quoted price.
- **Certificate of Origin ↔ FTAs**: links this lecture to the free-trade-agreement material (USMCA/T-MEC, rules of origin, HS tariff categories).
- **Factoring ↔ general finance**: a working-capital tool, conceptually parallel to LC-backed financing in that both convert uncertain future receipts into certain near-term cash.
