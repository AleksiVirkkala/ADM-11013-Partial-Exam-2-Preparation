## 3. Core concepts

### 3.1 Incoterms (recap)
- **Four groups:** E, F, C, D. **11 total** Incoterms. Current version: **Incoterms 2020** (revised every 10 years).
- Origin: **International Chamber of Commerce, Paris, 1930s** (pre-WWII; to facilitate international trade).
- **Group E — EXW (Ex Works):** seller delivers at own premises (warehouse/office); buyer bears everything after.
- **Group F:** seller delivers only up to the **port of the seller's country** (port of departure); buyer takes over from there.
- **Group C:** seller pays main carriage up to the **port of the buyer's country**.
- **Group D:** seller delivers all the way to the **buyer's inland location**.
- No legal requirement to use Incoterms; they are used because they prevent misunderstanding between parties from different legal systems and languages.
- Choice of Incoterm is driven by **bargaining power** (large buyer vs. small seller, or vice versa), not a fixed rule.
- Originally devised for maritime transport; in North America often used informally for land shipments (e.g., FOB Laredo, Texas) even though technically incorrect. On land, "port" can mean a **land port** (e.g., the Netherlands–Germany border, Laredo TX on the Rio Grande).

### 3.2 Letter of Credit (recap)
- Used when buyer and seller in different countries **do not know each other**; introduces a trusted bank intermediary.
- **Issuing bank = buyer's bank** (issues the L/C in favor of the seller/beneficiary).
- **Notifying (advising) bank = seller's bank**.
- If banks don't know each other, a correspondent/intermediary bank (e.g., Citibank) is used.
- Issuing bank pays only once all documents are verified correct; then forwards documents and payment through the notifying bank to credit the seller's account.
- Anchored to the **five necessary shipping documents** from the previous lecture.

### 3.3 Bill of Lading (recap)
- **Three functions** (exam-critical):
  1. **Document of title** — proof of legal possession of the merchandise (analogous to a car title: "título de propiedad"). Not easily replaced if lost.
  2. **Contract** between the shipper (exporter) and the transport company (carrier).
  3. **Receipt** of the goods having been loaded onto the transport.
- Carrier responds only to presentation of the original Bill of Lading — even if the buyer claims they paid, no BoL = no cargo.
- Carrier refuses to adjudicate buyer/seller disputes; parties must seek legal counsel.
- On land, also called a **waybill**; in Mexico: *carta porte* / *conocimiento de embarque*. Same instrument, different names.

### 3.4 Factoring (recap)
- Selling **accounts receivable** for immediate cash flow. Most small-business failures are due to lack of cash flow despite having receivables.
- Mechanism: complete work → invoice customer → send copy to factor → factor pays ~**90% within 24 hours** → factor collects 100% from customer → factor's margin = ~10% (minus agreed fees).
- Customer typically doesn't know the receivable was sold (confidential factoring).
- Used domestically and internationally; especially international.
- **Historical origin:** Low Countries / Netherlands — **Amsterdam, Rotterdam, Bruges**, and other **Hanseatic League** cities of the North Sea trading network.

### 3.5 Maquiladora (manufacturing under license, Mexican version) — [EXAM]
- **Year:** Program created in **1965** by the Mexican government — Mexico's first real opening to foreign investment.
- **Context:** Mexico was a **very closed, import-substitution economy** since the Lázaro Cárdenas era (1930s). No free-trade agreements; finished foreign goods couldn't be sold domestically.
- **Mechanism — an exception to the closed-economy rule:**
  - Foreign inputs (from US, Europe, Canada, Asia) enter **duty-free** through northern border cities (e.g., **Ciudad Juárez**), sealed in trucks.
  - Assembled in Mexico using **cheap Mexican labor** (predominantly female migrant workers; "paid terribly").
  - Final product **must be exported within one year**; cannot be sold in Mexico.
  - When re-imported into the US, the **tariff is levied only on the value added in Mexico** (mostly the labor component).
- **Impact:** triggered large female labor migration to the northern border; made Mexico a cheap supplier, primarily to the US (similar to European *Lohnveredelung* / "cheap sowing" arrangements with Eastern Europe post-1989).
- **Today:** less prominent because of broader trade liberalization (NAFTA/USMCA), but still exists.

### 3.7 Limitations of Licensing — Internalization Theory *(Book: p. 231, Ch. 8)*
- **Internalization theory** (also called the **market-imperfections approach**) seeks to explain **why firms often prefer FDI over licensing** — ownership internalizes value that a contract cannot secure.
- Spelling matters: **internalization**, not "internationalization."

**Three weaknesses of licensing (write these out for the exam):**
1. **Licensing may give away valuable technological know-how to a potential foreign competitor** (textbook examples: Matsushita and Sony). Illustrative question: would a European firm be cautious about a Chinese licensing partner? Instructor's point: yes, because IP protection is uncertain.
2. **Licensing does not give the firm tight control over production, marketing, and strategy** in the foreign country — control that may be required to maximize profitability. Important because firms may want to exploit differences in **factor costs** (as in a Maquiladora-style operation, producing only part of the product in a given country); a licensee would be unlikely to accept such coordination.
3. **The firm's competitive advantage may be based on management, marketing, and manufacturing capabilities that are not amenable to licensing** (i.e., firm-specific/tacit capabilities). Cultural factor: Japanese firms (e.g., **Toyota**) famously avoid licensing — they believe only Japanese employees can fully run their system.

### 3.8 When licensing is NOT a good option — Managerial Implications *(Book: Ch. 8, LO 8.6)*
- **Works well** in simple, standardized products easily franchised (e.g., pizza — "doesn't take Albert Einstein to make a pizza"; Papa John's example — franchisees are invisible to end customers).
- **Bad idea in:**
  1. **High-technology industries** — protecting firm-specific expertise is paramount.
  2. **Global oligopolies** — multinational firms must maintain tight coordination to compete (examples named in class: **Coca-Cola vs. Pepsi**, **Boeing vs. Airbus**, communications companies, luxury goods).
  3. *(Third category per user's exam-prep brief: **intense cost-pressure industries** — cf. §7 to-verify. Instructor did hint: "when there's a very tight, small margin, you can lose it easily" while discussing why companies avoid licensing.)*

### 3.9 Figure 8.4 — Decision tree for mode of entry *(Book)*
Instructor walked the class through the logic:
- **Transport costs and tariffs low?** → **Export.**
- **If high, is know-how amenable to licensing?** (i.e., low-tech like pizza) → **License.**
- **If know-how is complex, is tight control required?** → If yes, and know-how cannot be protected by a licensing contract → **FDI.**

## 4. Definitions / terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Incoterms | Standardized international commercial delivery terms (11 in total, organized in 4 groups: E, F, C, D), published by the International Chamber of Commerce, Paris; current edition 2020. |
| Letter of Credit | Bank-issued instrument guaranteeing payment from the buyer's bank (issuing bank) to the seller via the seller's bank (notifying/advising bank) upon presentation of correct shipping documents. |
| Bill of Lading (BoL) | Shipping document serving three functions: title (legal possession), contract (carrier–shipper), and receipt. On land: waybill / *carta porte*. |
| Factoring | Sale of accounts receivable to a third party (the factor) at a discount (~90%) to improve cash flow; factor collects the full amount from the customer. |
| Maquiladora | Mexican program (1965–) allowing duty-free import of foreign inputs for assembly in border cities, with mandatory re-export; US tariff levied only on Mexican value added. |
| USMCA | United States–Mexico–Canada Agreement (in force 2020), successor to NAFTA (1994). |
| Rules of Origin | Criteria determining which goods qualify for preferential tariff treatment under an FTA (e.g., USMCA auto rule: 75% regional content; 40–45% of parts made by ≥$16/hour labor). |
| Set-aside (US agricultural subsidy) | Government payment to farmers for *not* producing, allowing land to recover. |
| Modes of foreign market entry | The alternative methods by which a firm can serve a foreign market: exporting, franchising, licensing, turnkey, joint venture, FDI, portfolio investment. |
| FDI (Foreign Direct Investment) | Equity investment giving the investor **control** (typically ≥10% of capital) over the foreign operation. |
| Portfolio investment | Passive financial investment in foreign securities, *without* control. |
| Internalization theory / Market-imperfections approach | Theory explaining why firms prefer FDI over licensing: ownership internalizes value that arm's-length contracts cannot efficiently protect or coordinate. |
| Global oligopoly | Market with a small number of interdependent multinational competitors requiring tight strategic coordination (e.g., Coca-Cola/Pepsi, Boeing/Airbus). |
