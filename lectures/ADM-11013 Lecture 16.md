## 1. Overview
This lecture covers the six stages of regional economic integration (from Free Trade Area to Political/Complete Union) with examples, plus the mechanics that make international trade work in practice: rules of origin (the 60% rule), the universal 8-digit harmonized customs code, and the role of the customs broker. It closes with the three types of US agricultural subsidies that Mexico and other emerging markets complain about.

## 3. Core concepts

### 3.1 The six levels of regional economic integration

| # | Level | Defining addition | Example |
|---|-------|-------------------|---------|
| 1 | **Free Trade Area (FTA)** | Barriers to trade between members are eliminated. Each member keeps its **own** tariffs on non-member goods. Third-country goods regulated by **rules of origin**. | USMCA / TMEC (formerly NAFTA) |
| 2 | **Customs Union** | FTA **plus** harmonized external tariffs on goods from non-member (third) countries. | Few pure examples today — most have advanced to common market. (Historically the Andean Community.) |
| 3 | **Common Market** | Customs union **plus** free movement of factors of production (labor and capital), not just goods. | Mercosur (Brazil, Argentina, Uruguay, Paraguay, with associates) — though in practice bureaucratic barriers to labor mobility remain. |
| 4 | **Economic Union** | Common market **plus** coordination of economic policies: **exchange rate, inflation, fiscal deficit**. | Non-Eurozone EU countries (Hungary, Poland, Sweden, Denmark, Czech Republic, Romania, Bulgaria). |
| 5 | **Monetary Union** | Economic union **plus** a **common currency**. | Eurozone. |
| 6 | **Political / Complete Union** | One country with a central government. | Mexico (32 states) and the United States (50 states). |

**Note on the EU:** Economists often point out the EU is **not a complete fiscal union** — tax rates and policy are not harmonized. The US is also not a full fiscal union (states have different income taxes; some have no state income tax). Mexico, by contrast, is a complete fiscal union — income tax is the same across all 32 states, with no state income tax added.

### 3.2 Rules of origin and the 60% rule
The USMCA applies to **both tariff and non-tariff barriers**.

**Purpose of rules of origin:** to prevent **triangulation** — i.e., goods from a third country (e.g., Korea or China) being shipped into a member country's port, briefly warehoused (e.g., in Tijuana), re-labeled "Made in Mexico," and then exported into the US duty-free under the FTA.

**Threshold:** **60%** regional-content baseline to claim country of origin. Under the Trump-era renegotiation, the threshold for **automobiles was raised** above 60%.

**Enforcement:** The customs broker is responsible for declaring origin; national customs authorities (arm of the treasury) verify.

### 3.3 The 8-digit harmonized customs code
- Every good has a unique **8-digit harmonized customs code** — the system is universal across all countries.
- It **defines** what a good is for international-trade purposes (not the shipping label or description). Tariffs are applied based on this code.
- Originated at the **Customs Cooperation Council in Brussels, Belgium, after World War II**.
- Example given in class: footwear → code beginning **6401 / 6405** (subdivided further by material, e.g., leather vs. tennis vs. high heels).
- **Exceptions:** living humans have no customs code (anti-trafficking); cadavers, compressed air, and other gases **do** have codes.
- **Disputes** about classification (is this a pin or a pencil?) are resolved by reasoned interpretation of the catalog.

### 3.4 The customs broker
- A **private-sector intermediary** who handles all customs documentation for importers/exporters.
- Exists in essentially every country (Mexico, US, Hungary — "Vámházas" is one local term; Spanish: *agente aduanal*).
- Hiring one is not legally required in every case, but the paperwork (e.g., in Mexico) is complex enough that clients usually need one.
- Must apply the 8-digit code correctly; repeated mistakes can cost the broker their license (analogous to a lawyer being disbarred).
- Generally a legitimate profession, though isolated cases of extortion exist.
- **Five documents** (mentioned by teacher as material for the next class) are typically required for a Mexican import/export transaction.

### 3.5 Mexican customs "traffic-light" system
- Trucks entering Mexico press a button at the first customs checkpoint.
- **Green light** → pass through, no inspection.
- **Red light** → 100% inspection; goods offloaded and checked against documentation (correct customs code, etc.).
- A second checkpoint exists further into the interior (~30 km — see §7); a truck that got a green light passes straight through; a red light gets a second inspection chance.
- The system is being gradually relaxed under US pressure, but still exists. The US never had an equivalent.
- **Point:** in an FTA the members do **not** have to harmonize customs *procedures*. In a customs union they would.

### 3.6 Tariff vs. non-tariff barriers

**Tariff barriers** (monetary charges):
- **Ad valorem** — percentage of the invoice value (most common method).
- **Official prices** — tariff applied to an official catalog price rather than the invoice (Mexico used this historically for sensitive goods).
- **Specific** — a fixed amount per unit.
- **Combination** of the above.

**Non-tariff barriers:**
- **Quotas** (numerical limits, import or export).
- **Currency manipulation.**
- **Sanctions / embargoes** (e.g., US against Cuba; historical Mexico against Chile and South Africa).
- **Voluntary export restrictions** (e.g., OPEC petroleum quotas).
- **Import licenses.**
- **Subsidies.**
- **"Buy national" campaigns.**

### 3.7 US agricultural subsidies — the three types Mexico complains about

Emerging markets, including Mexico, complain about the agricultural subsidies of the US, Canada, and the EU. Mexico's largest trading partner by far is the US.

1. **Price floor (minimum price)**
   - The US Department of Agriculture guarantees a minimum price for a commodity (wheat, corn, sugar, etc.).
   - If the market price falls **below** the floor, the USDA pays the farmer the difference.
   - If the market price **exceeds** the floor, the farmer keeps the upside (no clawback).
   - **Effect:** guaranteed income → over-production → world price depressed → Mexican farmers receive less income for their output. Mexico has **no** comparable price-floor program.

2. **Set-aside / "fallow" program**
   - Every ~7–9 years (depending on product) the farmer is paid **not to produce** — the land is left fallow (wild).
   - Payment is based on the farmer's normal production on that land.
   - **Stated rationale:** soil conservation — continuous cultivation of cotton, sugar, wheat, or corn depletes nutrients; a fallow year re-enriches the soil.
   - **Side observation:** the US Midwest already over-produces so much that in autumn grain is stored in the streets for lack of silo capacity, further depressing world prices.
   - **EU analogue:** the EU pays farmers to convert land into forests for conservation — similar idea (reduce excess production, preserve land).

3. **Technological / technical assistance to the farmer**
   - US land-grant universities and agricultural extension programs send scientists to farms to advise on production methods.
   - Mexico now has **some** version of this (unlike the first two subsidies).

**Why did Mexico accept these subsidies in the USMCA negotiation?**
Because Mexico chose to fight other battles. The subsidy dispute is better handled via the WTO, and Mexico preferred to protect its position on tariffs and other higher-priority issues with its main trade partner. Mexico does not like the arrangement but is willing to live with it for now.

**Other Mexican agricultural subsidies mentioned by students (not the three "main" types):** water subsidies for rural areas, some seed subsidies, tax deductions on agricultural equipment.

## 4. Definitions / terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Free Trade Area (FTA)** | Economic bloc where barriers to trade between members are eliminated, but each member keeps its own tariffs on non-members. |
| **Customs Union** | FTA + harmonized external tariffs. |
| **Common Market** | Customs union + free movement of labor and capital. |
| **Economic Union** | Common market + coordination of exchange rates, inflation, and fiscal deficit. |
| **Monetary Union** | Economic union + a common currency. |
| **Political / Complete Union** | Integration into a single country with a central government. |
| **Fiscal Union** | A union in which tax rates and tax policy are harmonized (Mexico is; the EU and US are not). |
| **Rules of origin** | Criteria determining a good's country of origin to prevent duty-free "triangulation" of third-country goods through a member. |
| **60% rule** | Baseline regional-content threshold under USMCA (higher for autos post-Trump renegotiation). |
| **Triangulation** | Routing third-country goods (e.g., Chinese) through a member country (e.g., Mexico) to claim FTA origin. |
| **8-digit harmonized customs code** | Universal global code identifying every traded good; basis for tariff classification. |
| **Customs broker** | Private-sector intermediary who prepares customs documentation and applies the harmonized codes. |
| **Ad valorem tariff** | Tariff calculated as a percentage of invoice value. |
| **Specific tariff** | Tariff as a fixed amount per physical unit. |
| **Official prices** | Tariff based on an official catalog price rather than invoice. |
| **Quota** | Numerical ceiling on the quantity of a good imported or exported. |
| **Price floor** | Government-guaranteed minimum price; below-floor market prices trigger subsidy payment. |
| **Set-aside / fallow** | Program paying farmers not to produce for a year; soil-conservation rationale. |
| **Technical assistance** | Government-funded agricultural advisory/extension service (third type of US subsidy). |
| **Mercosur** | Common market of South America (Brazil, Argentina, Uruguay, Paraguay, with associates). |
| **USMCA / TMEC** | Treaty between Mexico, the US, and Canada; successor to NAFTA. |
| **Customs Cooperation Council (Brussels)** | Post-WWII body that designed the harmonized customs code system. |
