## 1. Overview
The lecture zooms in on the mechanics of exporting under NAFTA (now T-MEC/USMCA): how goods are classified via the 8-digit harmonized customs code, how tariffs were phased out in categories A/B/C/C+, how *rules of origin* prevent trampoline imports, and the step-by-step documentary flow of a real trade transaction. ## 3. Core concepts

### 3.1 The 8-digit harmonized customs code
Every good has a customs code (except living humans; a cadaver has one). The harmonized system is the same internationally — the first digits identify broad categories (e.g., oils/fats of animal or vegetable origin), then 2 more, then 4 more (sub-parts). To determine a tariff under any FTA (NAFTA, EU agreements, etc.), you **need the 8-digit harmonized code** plus any exceptions.

### 3.2 Mexico's pre-NAFTA context (background, not exam-core)
- Until ~1989 Mexico followed an *autarky* (independence) model; average tariff ~27%, lowered to ~10% in 1989.
- Import licenses required for nearly everything in the 1980s.
- Tariffs were charged on an *official price list*, typically higher than invoice, to amplify duties.
- Joined GATT in 1986. President Salinas de Gortari tried to pivot to Europe in the early 1990s but Europe was focused on Eastern Europe opening up — so Mexico turned to the US/Canada.

### 3.3 NAFTA (1994) — scope and objectives
- Entered into force **1 January 1994**; 22 chapters. Renamed USMCA / T-MEC in 2018 (often still said "NAFTA" in class).
- Objectives: **eliminate trade barriers** (tariff and non-tariff), **protect intellectual property rights**, **increase investment opportunities**, **promote free and just trade**, **facilitate resolution of controversies**.
- Outcome: barriers were vastly **reduced** (not fully eliminated).

### 3.4 Three US agricultural subsidies (EXAM)
Mexico complains bitterly about three classes of US farm subsidies:
1. **Price floor** — a minimum guaranteed price. If e.g. the wheat market price falls below the floor, the government pays the farmer the difference.
2. **Set-aside program** — the government pays farmers *not* to produce for one year every 7th or 8th year (varies by product), ostensibly to let the soil recover.
3. **Technical assistance** — research and extension support from agricultural and scientific universities. Mexico and Europe have some version of this, but the US has much more.

(Europe's subsidy scheme is different — more oriented toward environmental conservation and limiting production.)

### 3.5 NAFTA tariff-removal mechanics: categories A, B, C, C+
Every 8-digit customs code was negotiated into one of four categories, **separately for each of the three countries** (the same good could be A in Canada, B in the US, C in Mexico). The more sensitive the good to the domestic economy, the slower the removal.

| Category | Removal schedule | Finished |
|---|---|---|
| A | Tariff removed **immediately** on 1 Jan 1994 | 1994 |
| B | Gradual over 5 years (1/5 per year) | 1 Jan 1998 |
| C | Gradual over 10 years (10% per year) | 1 Jan 2004 |
| C+ | Gradual over **15 years** — most sensitive products (corn, beans, automobiles, textiles) | 2008 |

**Worked example — category B, starting tariff 20%**: 20 → 16 → 12 → 8 → 4 → 0 (1/5 removed each year).

**How countries differed (by invoice value of exports, not just code count):**

| Flow | Share in category A (immediate) |
|---|---|
| Mexico → US | ~84% |
| Mexico → Canada | ~79% |
| US → Mexico | ~43% |
| Canada → Mexico | ~41% |

Teacher's interpretive point: counting *codes* alone was a "half-truth" — code counts can hide that low-value items (erasers, staples, folders) sat in A while high-value technology items sat in C. What matters is *value* (non-petroleum invoicing). By value the US and Canada opened up far more immediately than Mexico did, because Mexico needed time to become competitive — a pragmatic concession, not pure charity (to avoid industrial collapse and a migration wave).

**Political demagoguery example (Lopez Obrador, 2008):** claimed the 1 Jan 2008 removal of C+ tariffs on corn/beans/automobiles would destroy Mexican agriculture and industry. The flaw: those tariffs had been phased down over 15 years (1/15 per year); on 1 Jan 2008 only the final 1/15 was removed.

### 3.6 FTA vs. Customs Union (EXAM)
- **Free Trade Agreement (FTA)** — e.g., NAFTA/USMCA: member countries remove tariffs *among themselves*, but each keeps its *own* external tariff against third parties (e.g., China). This is the "kindergarten" level of integration.
- **Customs Union** — members not only free trade among themselves but also **harmonize external tariffs** against third-party countries. Example: if North America were a customs union, the tariff on Chinese imports would have to be identical in the US, Canada, and Mexico.

### 3.7 Rules of origin (EXAM)
**Purpose:** prevent third-country firms from using a member country as a *trampoline*. Example: a Korean firm ships socks to a California warehouse, labels them "Made in USA," then exports tariff-free to Mexico under NAFTA. Rules of origin block this by requiring a minimum share of regional value.

**Two formulas (Regional Value Content, RVC):**

**Method 1 — Transaction Value (FOB basis):**
\[
\text{RVC} = \frac{TV - VNM}{TV} \times 100
\]
- Threshold: **> 60%** → the good enters as a regional product (tariff-free).
- `TV` = Transaction Value, **FOB** (Free On Board — i.e., *excluding* international insurance and freight).
- `VNM` = Value of Non-originating Materials (materials used in production that come from outside the FTA).

**Method 2 — Net Cost:**
\[
\text{RVC} = \frac{NC - VNM}{NC} \times 100
\]
- Threshold: **> 50%** → good enters as regional (used when the FOB formula fails).
- `NC` = Net Cost (excludes freight and insurance).
- `VNM` = as above.

**Expertise:** the **customs broker** (and the customs officer) is the specialist who applies these formulas.

**2018 exception — automobiles:** Trump's renegotiation raised the automobile origin threshold to **75%**, responding to his claim (disputed) that Mexico was assembling Chinese parts and re-exporting as "Made in Mexico."

### 3.8 Incoterms (brief)
**FOB (Free On Board)** — price *excludes* international insurance and freight (used in the Transaction Value formula). Full coverage of Incoterms is promised for a later class.

### 3.9 Flow of a trade transaction (EXAM) — 8 steps
Using Mexico as the example; the same principles apply elsewhere (EU included).

1. **Negotiation** between importer and exporter on the *four P's* (Product, Price, Place/delivery, Promotion/publicity) and contract details.
2. The Mexican party **chooses a customs agent** (customs broker) or assigns an internal employee to facilitate the transaction.
3. The **customs agent prepares the Pedimento** (the Mexican import/export declaration — the "tax document") and reviews it with the client. Accuracy is critical — errors (e.g., wrong customs code) can cost the broker their licence.
4. The **exporter prepares the Certificate of Origin** (certifying the country of origin), **Packing List** (what's in each box), and **Invoice (factura)**, and sends copies to the importer and the importer's customs agent.
5. The exporter / carrier prepares the **Bill of Lading** (*guía* / *conocimiento de embarque*) with its **three functions**:
   - Document of **legal title / possession** (proves you are not a pirate if intercepted at sea).
   - **Contract** between shipper and carrier.
   - **Delivery receipt**.
   The Bill of Lading is heavily used in letters of credit.
6. **Merchandise is shipped** to the customs of the importing country.
7. The **customs agent pays duties and taxes** on behalf of the client and receives a stamp on the Pedimento documenting that all taxes have been paid.
8. **Customs of the importing country compares the physical merchandise against the import documents** (Pedimento, Certificate of Origin, Packing List, Invoice, Bill of Lading) for verification.

### 3.10 Mexico's web of FTAs (general culture, not exam)
Mexico has the largest number of FTAs of any country — North America (USMCA), European Union, EFTA (Norway, Liechtenstein, Iceland, Switzerland), Central America (Honduras, El Salvador, etc.), Israel, Colombia, Chile, Uruguay, Japan, Bolivia, Peru, Panama. Opening strategy began under Miguel de la Madrid and accelerated under Salinas de Gortari.

## 4. Definitions / terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| 8-digit harmonized code | Internationally standardized customs classification identifying any tradable good; required to look up a tariff. |
| Autarky | Economic model of national self-sufficiency / independence (Mexico's pre-1989 posture). |
| NAFTA / USMCA / T-MEC | North American Free Trade Agreement (1994, 22 chapters); renamed USMCA (English) / T-MEC (Spanish) in 2018. |
| Category A / B / C / C+ | NAFTA tariff-removal schedules: immediate / 5-year / 10-year / 15-year (to 2008). |
| FTA | Free Trade Agreement — tariffs removed *among* members; each keeps its own external tariff. |
| Customs Union | FTA + harmonized **external** tariffs against third countries. |
| Rules of Origin | Criteria determining whether a good qualifies as regional, preventing third-country trampoline exports. |
| RVC | Regional Value Content — percentage of a good's value originating within the FTA region. |
| TV (Transaction Value) | Invoice value of the good on an FOB basis (excl. international insurance and freight). |
| NC (Net Cost) | Cost of producing the good excluding freight and insurance. |
| VNM | Value of Non-originating Materials (inputs from outside the FTA). |
| FOB | *Free On Board* — Incoterm; price excludes international insurance and freight. |
| Pedimento | Mexican import/export declaration / tax document filed by the customs broker. |
| Certificate of Origin | Document certifying the country of origin of the goods. |
| Packing List | Document listing the contents of each shipping unit. |
| Invoice (factura) | Commercial bill for the exported goods. |
| Bill of Lading (guía / conocimiento de embarque) | Transport document with three functions: legal title, contract, delivery receipt. |
| Customs broker | Licensed specialist who prepares the Pedimento, applies rules of origin, and pays duties. |
| Letter of credit | Bank-issued credit instrument used in international trade (detailed in a later class). |
| Factoring | Purchase of a firm's accounts receivable at a discount (e.g., 95% for 100% face value); common in international trade, originated in Paris. |
| Price floor | US farm subsidy: guaranteed minimum price; government pays the gap if the market price falls below. |
| Set-aside program | US farm subsidy: government pays farmers not to produce for one year every 7–8 years. |
| Technical assistance (subsidy) | US farm support via agricultural/scientific universities. |
| Four P's | Product, Price, Place (delivery), Promotion/Publicity — negotiated in step 1 of a trade transaction. |

## 5. Worked examples / exercises

**Category-B phase-out, starting tariff 20%, over 5 years** (1/5 removed per year):

| Year | Tariff |
|------|--------|
| 1994 | 16% |
| 1995 | 12% |
| 1996 | 8% |
| 1997 | 4% |
| 1998 | 0% |

**RVC — Transaction Value (numerical illustration, constructed from the formula):**
Suppose socks exported from the US to Mexico have `TV = $100` (FOB) and `VNM = $35` (imported Korean yarn and trim).
\[
\text{RVC} = \frac{100 - 35}{100} \times 100 = 65\%
\]
Since 65% > 60%, the socks qualify as regional and enter Mexico tariff-free under NAFTA.

**RVC — Net Cost (numerical illustration):**
If `NC = $80` and `VNM = $35`:
\[
\text{RVC} = \frac{80 - 35}{80} \times 100 = 56.25\%
\]
56.25% > 50%, so the good qualifies via the Net Cost method even if it fails the 60% FOB test.

*(The transcript does not give worked numbers — these illustrations apply the formulas as given in the notes.)*
