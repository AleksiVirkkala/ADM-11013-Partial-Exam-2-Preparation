# T05 — Export Costing (Direct vs Absorption; Dumping)

**Spanish label:** Exportaciones — Costeo
**Appears in lectures:** L15
**Textbook:** Not explicitly cited in class (exercise sourced from Bancomext executive-education material); connect to Hill Ch. 7 on dumping / anti-dumping policy.
**Exam priority:** MEDIUM–HIGH — the teacher spent a full class on this, flagged both "dumping" and "direct vs. absorbent cost" as explicit `[EXAM]` items, and hinted at bonus points for the worked exercise.

## 1. TL;DR

An export manager can cost a product two ways. **Direct (variable) costing** assigns only variable costs per unit and treats fixed costs as period costs — so any export price above variable cost is accretive at the margin. **Absorbent (full) costing** spreads fixed + variable costs over every unit, giving a higher break-even floor. Direct costing lets you price aggressively abroad but exposes the firm to **dumping** accusations, because foreign regulators compare the export price to full cost or home-market price. In the L15 exercise (Super Brothers), domestic sales of 30,000 units at $2,000 leave a $23M shortfall against $83M total cost; the remaining 10,000 export units must fetch **$2,300/unit** to break even on absorption, but any price above **$900/unit** is already profitable on direct-cost logic.

## 2. Direct cost (variable costing)

- **Definition:** Costing method that assigns only **variable costs** (materials, variable manufacturing, variable operating, and — in the US — labor) to the product. Fixed costs are treated as period expenses and are *not* allocated per unit.
- **When used:**
  - Incremental / marginal decisions: "Should we accept this extra export order?"
  - Short-run export pricing when fixed costs are already being paid by domestic sales.
  - "Icing on the cake" logic — export volume is incremental, home market is what really matters.
- **Implication for export pricing:** any offered price **above variable cost** contributes positively at the margin. Every dollar above variable cost goes first to covering fixed costs that are already sunk, then to profit.
- **Risk:** the lower floor price makes the firm vulnerable to **dumping** accusations abroad (see §4).

## 3. Absorbent cost (absorption / full costing)

- **Definition:** Costing method under which each unit **absorbs both fixed and variable costs**. Fixed costs are allocated per unit using the **fixed rate of application**:

  $$\text{Fixed rate of application} = \frac{\text{Total fixed cost}}{\text{Normal production capacity}}$$

- **When used:**
  - Standard / long-run pricing decisions.
  - Break-even analysis — the price that covers **all** costs with no profit and no loss.
  - When the firm wants to insulate itself from dumping accusations (export price then closely tracks home price).
- **Implication for export pricing:** yields a **higher** floor price than direct costing. Matches what the firm needs to charge to recover total costs across its full output.
- **Trap / decoy:** the per-unit fixed rate ($500/unit in the exercise) and the per-hour fixed labor rate ($400/hour) *look* like costs that scale with output — they do **not**. Total fixed cost is fixed whether the firm produces 1 unit or 1,000,000.

## 4. Dumping

- **Definition:** Selling a product in a **foreign market below the (home-market or cost-based) market price**, typically as a short-term predatory strategy — accept losses now to destroy competitors, then raise prices once rivals have left.
- **WTO stance:** dumping is prohibited under WTO rules. Importing governments can impose **anti-dumping duties** (tariffs on the suspect product) once dumping is proven.
- **Why direct costing looks like dumping:** under direct costing the export floor price only needs to cover variable cost, so it can easily fall below the home-market absorbent price. Foreign authorities compare export price against home price or full cost and see a below-cost/below-home sale → dumping accusation.
- **Rule of thumb from the lecture:** **direct-cost pricing is the method most exposed to dumping accusations**; absorbent-cost pricing is much safer.
- **Real-world examples mentioned:** **CEMEX** (Mexican cement) repeatedly accused of dumping; Mexican glass producers (likely **Vitro**) similarly accused. Mexican firms are not uniquely targeted — many firms worldwide face dumping complaints.

## 5. Worked example (L15 — Super Brothers)

### 5.1 Data (useful vs. decoy)

| Item | Figure | Useful? |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Variable cost per unit | **$900/unit** | Yes — direct-cost key input |
| Variable operating cost | $100/unit | Ambiguous — see §9 |
| Fixed manufacturing | $25,000,000 | Yes (total) |
| Normal capacity (manufacturing) | 50,000 units/month | Denominator for rate |
| Fixed manufacturing rate | $500/unit | **Decoy** — looks per-unit but totals don't scale |
| Fixed labor | $20,000,000 | Yes (Mexico = fixed) |
| Normal labor capacity | 50,000 hours | Denominator for rate |
| Fixed labor rate | $400/hour | **Decoy** — looks per-hour but totals don't scale |
| Fixed operating | $2,000,000/month | Yes |
| Domestic sales (current) | 30,000 units @ $2,000/unit | Yes |
| Export offer | 10,000 units @ $1,300/unit | Yes |

### 5.2 Consolidated cost structure at 40,000 units produced

| Item | Amount |
| --- | --- |
| Total variable cost (40,000 × $900) | **$36,000,000** |
| Fixed manufacturing | $25,000,000 |
| Fixed labor | $20,000,000 |
| Fixed operating | $2,000,000 |
| **Total fixed** | **$47,000,000** |
| **Total cost** | **$83,000,000** |

Revenue already realised on the domestic market:

$$30{,}000 \times 2{,}000 = \$60{,}000{,}000$$

### 5.3 Side-by-side reasoning on the $1,300 export offer

| Step | Direct (variable) costing | Absorbent (full) costing |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Relevant per-unit cost | **$900** (variable only) | **$2,300** (break-even, see below) |
| Offered price | $1,300 | $1,300 |
| Gap per unit | $1,300 − $900 = **+$400** | $1,300 − $2,300 = **−$1,000** |
| Total on 10,000-unit order | **+$4,000,000** marginal gain | **−$10,000,000** unabsorbed loss |
| Decision | **ACCEPT** — adds $4M to profit | **REJECT** — booked as loss |

### 5.4 Absorbent break-even price — step by step

1. **Total cost to recover** at 40,000 units = **$83,000,000**.
2. **Revenue already secured** from 30,000 domestic units = 30,000 × $2,000 = **$60,000,000**.
3. **Shortfall** that the 10,000 export units must cover:

   $$83{,}000{,}000 - 60{,}000{,}000 = \$23{,}000{,}000$$

4. **Minimum break-even export price:**

   $$\frac{\$23{,}000{,}000}{10{,}000 \text{ units}} = \boxed{\$2{,}300 / \text{unit}}$$

At $2,300/unit the firm clears total cost exactly — no profit, no loss, warehouse empty.

### 5.5 Dumping angle on this exercise

- Export price $1,300 is **below** domestic price $2,000 **and below** absorbent break-even $2,300.
- A foreign regulator comparing the export price to either the home price or full cost would see a textbook below-cost sale → dumping complaint likely.
- Had the firm used absorbent costing to set the floor, the export price would sit near $2,300 — no dumping flag.

### 5.6 Decision exercises

- **Offer at $1,300/unit:**
  - Direct cost: accept — $400/unit marginal gain, +$4M total. Fixed costs are already covered by domestic sales anyway.
  - Absorbent cost: reject — $1,000/unit below break-even, −$10M of unabsorbed cost.
  - Dumping: high risk (price < home price < absorbent cost).
- **Offer at $2,500/unit:**
  - Direct cost: accept — $1,600/unit marginal gain, +$16M total.
  - Absorbent cost: accept — $200/unit above break-even, +$2M profit on the export block.
  - Dumping: low risk — export price **above** the domestic $2,000 price and above full cost.
- **Offer at exactly $900/unit:** break-even on direct cost (zero marginal contribution) — take only if there is a strategic reason (market entry, clearing stock). Big dumping flag.

## 6. Key terminology

| Term | Definition |
| --- | --- |
| Direct cost (variable costing) | Costing method assigning only variable costs per unit; fixed costs treated as period expenses. |
| Absorbent cost (absorption / full costing) | Costing method under which each unit absorbs both fixed and variable costs. |
| Variable cost | Cost that scales with units produced (materials, variable manufacturing/operating, US-style labor). |
| Fixed cost | Cost independent of output volume within the relevant range (plant overhead, property tax, Mexican labor). |
| Fixed rate of application | Total fixed cost ÷ normal production capacity — per-unit/per-hour absorption rate. |
| Normal production capacity | Standard expected output used as the denominator when absorbing fixed costs. |
| Marginal gain / loss | Incremental profit or loss from an additional order, using only incremental (variable) costs vs. incremental revenue. |
| Break-even price | Price at which total revenue equals total cost — no profit, no loss. |
| Dumping | Selling a product in a foreign market below market price (often below full cost) for short-term strategic reasons. |
| Anti-dumping duty | Government-imposed tariff on imports deemed to be dumped; WTO-sanctioned response. |
| Decoy data | In a cost exercise, figures that *look* per-unit/per-hour but refer to non-scaling fixed totals — a trap the student must spot. |

## 9. Watch out

- **Variable-cost composition is ambiguous.** The lecture uses **$900/unit** as the total variable cost in both direct-cost and absorbent-cost calculations, but the data sheet *also* lists a **$100/unit variable operating cost**. If that $100 is additional, variable cost becomes $1,000/unit, marginal gain drops from $400 to $300/unit, and total gain on the order becomes $3M instead of $4M. **Confirm with the teacher or classmates whether $900 already includes the $100 variable operating.**
- **"Dumping" in class ≠ strict WTO definition.** The teacher's framing ("selling below market price… short-term strategy to destroy competitors") is the business-usage definition. The WTO legal test is narrower: export price below "normal value" (typically home-market price in the ordinary course of trade). On the exam, the business framing is probably what's expected.
- **Fixed labor is fixed in Mexico, variable in the US.** If an exam question specifies a US-based firm, labor may need to be treated as variable — flipping part of the $20M from fixed to variable and changing both costings.
- **Fixed rates of application are decoys.** Do NOT multiply $500/unit × actual output as if it were a marginal cost. Total fixed cost doesn't move with output — only the per-unit *absorbed* portion does (and under-absorption occurs when output < normal capacity).
- **Break-even is "clear the warehouse" pricing, not a target price.** The $2,300/unit figure is the floor under absorbent logic, not a recommended selling price.
