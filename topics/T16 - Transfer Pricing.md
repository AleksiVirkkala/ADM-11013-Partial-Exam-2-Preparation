## 1. TL;DR
Multinational corporations (MNCs) use **four mechanisms** to shift *reported* profit from high-tax to low-tax jurisdictions and lower the group's combined tax bill: **(1) charging dividends**, **(2) charging royalties on IP**, **(3) transfer pricing proper** (manipulating intra-company prices), and **(4) fronting loans** routed through an intermediary bank. The strategy is legal in form but sits in a grey area; firms avoid making the shift blatant because zero profit in a high-tax country triggers audits. Hill Ch. 20.

## 2. The basic idea

### 2.1 Why MNCs engage in tax planning
- Different jurisdictions have very different corporate tax rates (e.g., Mexico 30%, USA federal 21%, Ireland 12.5%, Bermuda/Cayman/Jersey 0%).
- Shifting *reported* profit to a low-tax jurisdiction lowers the group's total tax bill and raises combined net profit.
- Legal in form, but tax authorities scrutinize — so firms target a **grey area**: trim high-tax profit by 5–10%, never to zero. Zero profit in a high-tax country is "very flagrant" and attracts an audit.
- No global harmonization exists. Proposals like Biden's push for a global minimum corporate tax and the OECD's 15% deal have not been fully enforced. The EU is a monetary/trade union, **not a fiscal union** — hence Germany cannot force Ireland to raise its 12.5% rate.

### 2.2 Tax haven 3-country structure
- **Country A** — actual operations, high tax (e.g., USA 40%).
- **Country B** — paper/shell office in a tax haven, low or 0% tax (e.g., Bermuda).
- **Country C** — the customer country, high tax (e.g., UK 50%).
- A sells to B **at cost** (no profit in A). B resells to C at a high price (all profit parks in B at 0%). C sells to the public at cost (no profit in C).
- **All profit is domiciled in the tax haven.** Bermuda collects nothing in corporate tax but benefits via employment, personal income tax, and fees.

## 3. The FOUR mechanisms (MEMORIZE for exam)

> [EXAM] Teacher verbatim: *"Explain to me the four methods… That's how it works."* / *"this will be a question of the final exam. What are the mechanisms? Explain each one."* Hill Ch. 20.

### 3.1 Charging dividends
- Structure the **low-tax subsidiary as the parent/owner** of the high-tax subsidiary.
- The high-tax subsidiary pays **dividends up** to the low-tax parent on its profits.
- Example: **Microsoft Ireland owns Microsoft Mexico.** Mexico's after-tax profits flow as dividends to Ireland, where they are taxed at only 12.5%.
- Effect: cash/profit moves from high-tax jurisdiction → low-tax jurisdiction.

### 3.2 Charging royalties on IP
- Place patents, brands, and copyrights in the **low-tax subsidiary**.
- The high-tax subsidiary must pay **royalties** to the low-tax parent to use those patents/brands when it sells products.
- Effect: **expenses go up in the high-tax country** (reducing taxable income there); **income goes up in the low-tax country** (taxed at the lower rate).

### 3.3 Transfer pricing (proper)
- Artificially **raise or lower the intra-company price** of goods or services between sister subsidiaries.
- Not based on market pricing — based on tax objectives.
- Example: a high-tax subsidiary buys inputs from a low-tax sibling at **inflated prices**. Expense ↑ in the high-tax country; income ↑ in the low-tax country.
- See §4.1 for the two-country worked example.

### 3.4 Fronting loans via an intermediary bank
- A **disguised intra-group loan routed through an unrelated bank** so the tax authorities do not see a direct parent–subsidiary financing relationship.
- Structure:

```
Subsidiary in TAX HAVEN          Intermediary BANK          Subsidiary in HIGH-TAX country
(e.g., Bermuda, 0%)   ──────►   (e.g., Barclays)  ──────►  (e.g., UK, 50%)
       deposits $50M                                         receives $50M loan
       earns 9% interest  ◄──── pays 9% ◄───  pays 9–10% ◄───  pays interest on loan
```

- The tax-haven subsidiary deposits cash at the bank and earns interest (taxed at 0%).
- The bank **lends the same money** to the high-tax subsidiary, which pays interest to the bank.
- **Why the bank in the middle?** A direct loan between the two subsidiaries would alert tax authorities. The bank obscures the intra-firm nature — the firms deliberately make the structure complex to pass audits.
- Net effect: interest is a **deductible expense in the high-tax country** (reducing taxable income) and **interest income in the tax haven** (taxed at 0%).

## 4. Worked examples

### 4.1 Two-country transfer pricing
Country A marginal tax = 40%, Country B = 50%. One MNC owns both.

**Without strategy** — A sells to B at $10,000; B sells to public at $12,000.
- A: profit $2,000 → tax $800 → net $1,200
- B: profit $2,000 → tax $1,000 → net $1,000
- **Combined net profit = $2,200**

**With strategy** — A raises intra-company price to B from $10,000 → $12,000.
- A: profit $4,000 → tax $1,600 → net $2,400
- B: profit $0 → tax $0 → net $0
- **Combined net profit = $2,400. Saving = $200.** (Illustrative; real numbers are in millions and not this flagrant.)

### 4.2 Three-country tax-haven chain (US → Bermuda → UK)
USA (40%) → Bermuda paper entity (0%) → UK (50%).
- US sells to Bermuda at cost ($8,000). Bermuda resells to UK at $12,000. UK sells to public at $12,000.
- Profit: US $0, Bermuda $4,000, UK $0. Total tax = $0. **All $4,000 profit parked in Bermuda at 0%.**

### 4.3 Fronting loan — numerical example
- **Bermuda subsidiary**: deposits **$50M** at 9% → earns **$4.5M** interest, taxed at 0% → keeps all $4.5M.
- **UK subsidiary**: borrows $50M from the bank at 9–10% → pays **$4.5M** interest → deductible expense → reduces UK taxable income by $4.5M.
- **Bank**: pockets a thin spread, provides legal distance between the two subsidiaries.
- **Net**: $4.5M of income shifted from UK (50% tax) to Bermuda (0% tax) → group saves the UK corporate rate × $4.5M.

## 5. Broader context
- **OECD BEPS** (Base Erosion and Profit Shifting) project targets these practices.
- **2021 Global Minimum Tax** agreement — corporate tax floor of 15% — still enforced inconsistently.
- Firms doing this in practice (teacher's named examples): **Apple, Nike, Uber, Starbucks, Amazon**.
  - **Uber Mexico**: invoices carry an Amsterdam address → Mexican revenue is channelled to the Netherlands subsidiary.
  - **Apple / "Double Irish Dutch Sandwich"**: Irish subsidiary received payments from Apple subs across Europe/Middle East/outside US; by 2020 Apple had **$252B cash parked in Ireland** (pop. ~5.5M). Apple further routed Irish money through a **Jersey (0%)** subsidiary that charged royalties and dividends worldwide.
- Principal tax havens to recognize: Ireland (12.5%), Netherlands (25% with favourable structures), Luxembourg, Switzerland, Bahamas, Cayman Islands, BVI, Bermuda, Panama, Belize, Hong Kong (16.5%), Singapore, Channel Islands (Jersey/Guernsey/Sark — 0%).
- **Mexico is not a tax haven** — local phrase: it is "a tax jail."

## 6. Key terminology
| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| MNC | Multinational Corporation — firm operating subsidiaries in multiple countries |
| Transfer pricing | Internal price charged on intra-company transactions between sister subsidiaries |
| Transfer pricing strategy | Deliberate raising/lowering of intra-company prices to shift profit to low-tax jurisdictions |
| Tax haven | Jurisdiction with very low or zero corporate tax (often with secrecy) used to domicile profits |
| Shell corporation / paper entity | Subsidiary with no real factory or customers — only an office and invoices — used as a pass-through |
| Pass-through entity | Intermediate subsidiary through which sales are invoiced to shift profit |
| Fronting loan | Intra-group loan routed via an unrelated bank to obscure the parent–subsidiary financing link |
| Charging dividends (mechanism 1) | Low-tax parent owns high-tax subsidiary, which pays dividends up |
| Charging royalties (mechanism 2) | Placing IP in the low-tax subsidiary, which charges royalties to high-tax subsidiaries |
| Double Irish (Dutch Sandwich) | Apple-era structure routing profits through Ireland → Netherlands → Jersey (0%) |
| BEPS | Base Erosion and Profit Shifting — OECD initiative against these practices |
| SAT | *Servicio de Administración Tributaria* — Mexico's federal tax authority |
| Fiscal union | A union with harmonized taxation (the EU is **not** one) |
