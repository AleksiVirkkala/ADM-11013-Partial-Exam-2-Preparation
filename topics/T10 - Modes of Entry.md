# T10 — Modes of Foreign Market Entry

**Spanish label:** Modalidades de entrada a mercados extranjeros
**Appears in lectures:** L20, L21
**Textbook:** Hill Ch. 15 (Entering Foreign Markets); cross-referenced from Ch. 8 (FDI) — verify edition for exact pages
**Exam priority:** HIGH — teacher explicitly flagged "list the modes of entry and briefly describe each" and "pros and cons of each mode" as 2nd-partial-exam questions; the **Maquiladora** is an additional explicit exam item.

## 1. TL;DR
A firm serving a foreign market can choose among **6 canonical modes of entry**, arranged from lightest commitment to heaviest: **Export/Import → Manufacturing under License → Franchising → Turnkey Project → Strategic Alliance / Joint Venture → FDI**. These six feed directly into the FDI-vs-licensing decision ([T11](T11%20-%20FDI%20vs%20Licensing.md)) and the three FDI theories ([T12](T12%20-%20FDI%20Theories.md)). The **Maquiladora** is Mexico's historical variant of manufacturing under license. **Portfolio investment** is a passive financial activity (BoP capital account, no operational presence) and the teacher does **not** count it as a mode of entry — treat it as a foundational BoP concept, not a mode.

> **Count to write on the exam: 6.** The teacher consistently says six modes in class; the Lecture 21 notes are explicit. Some editions of Hill list portfolio investment separately as a seventh — ignore that for this course.

## 2. The 6 modes

### 2.1 Export / Import
- **Mechanism:** cross-border sale (export) or purchase (import) of finished goods and services; no foreign production presence.
- **Pros:** lowest commitment, lowest capital outlay, quick way to test a market.
- **Cons:** exposed to **transport costs** (kills low value-to-weight products — e.g. CEMEX cement) and **tariffs / trade barriers**, which can push the firm toward FDI ([T11 §3.1](T11%20-%20FDI%20vs%20Licensing.md)).

### 2.2 Foreign Direct Investment (FDI)
- **Mechanism:** investment that gives **control** (typically **≥10% equity**) over a foreign operation; delivered as **greenfield** (build-from-scratch) or **acquisition / brownfield**.
- **Pros:** full control over know-how, strategy, and profits; can internalize intangibles that can't be licensed.
- **Cons:** highest capital commitment and political/operational risk.
- **Deeper coverage:** why firms choose FDI over licensing → [T11](T11%20-%20FDI%20vs%20Licensing.md); theoretical drivers (internalization, strategic behavior, Dunning's eclectic paradigm) → [T12](T12%20-%20FDI%20Theories.md); benefits & costs → [T13](T13%20-%20Benefits%20and%20Costs%20of%20FDI.md).

### 2.3 Turnkey Project
- **Mechanism:** a contractor **designs, builds, trains local staff, and hands over** a ready-to-operate facility ("turn the key") to a foreign client.
- **Typical sectors:** **chemical, petroleum refining, pharmaceuticals, heavy engineering, power plants** — large capital projects where the contractor's expertise is the product.
- **Pros:** earn revenue in otherwise closed markets (e.g., where FDI restrictions block foreign ownership); no long-run political risk.
- **Cons:** **no long-term presence** in the host market; the firm may **create a future competitor** by transferring know-how.

### 2.4 Manufacturing under License
- **Mechanism:** a **licensor** grants a foreign **licensee** the right to use its **technology, patents, or brand** to manufacture locally, in exchange for **royalties / fees**.
- **Pros:** low capital commitment; a way to monetize IP where exporting is blocked by tariffs/transport.
- **Cons:** the three canonical weaknesses ([T11](T11%20-%20FDI%20vs%20Licensing.md)): (1) giving away know-how to a potential competitor, (2) loss of tight control over production and strategy, (3) cannot transfer firm-specific management/marketing/manufacturing capabilities.

#### 2.4.1 Maquiladora (Mexican special case — [EXAM])
- **Established 1965** by the Mexican government — Mexico's **first real opening to foreign investment** under an otherwise **closed, import-substitution economy** (rooted in the Lázaro Cárdenas era, 1930s).
- **Mechanism — an exception to the closed-economy rule:**
  - Foreign **inputs enter duty-free** through northern border cities (e.g., **Ciudad Juárez, Tijuana**), sealed in trucks.
  - Assembled in Mexico using **cheap Mexican labor** (predominantly **female migrant workers** who moved to the border towns — "paid terribly").
  - Final product **must be re-exported within one year**; cannot be sold domestically in Mexico.
  - When re-imported into the US, the **US tariff is levied only on the Mexican value added** (essentially the labor component) — analogous to European *Lohnveredelung* (contract-manufacturing with Eastern Europe after 1989).
- **Impact:** created Mexico's northern-border industrial belt; made Mexico a cheap supplier, overwhelmingly to the US.
- **Today:** largely superseded by **NAFTA (1994) / USMCA-T-MEC (2020)**, which opened trade broadly, but the Maquiladora legal framework still exists.

### 2.6 Franchising
- **Mechanism:** a specialized form of licensing — licensor grants the use of its **brand and business system** plus **stringent operating rules** (recipes, décor, service standards, training, supply chain) and ongoing support.
- **Works best for:** simple, replicable, codifiable products and services — **fast food** (McDonald's, Subway, Papa John's), hotel chains, rental cars.
- **Pros:** rapid global expansion without huge capital outlay by the franchisor; local owners carry operational risk.
- **Cons:** brand damage if a franchisee underperforms; franchisor/franchisee tension (e.g., teacher's *7 Brew* Iowa anecdote — the franchisor pressures expansion or claws back the territory).

### 2.7 Strategic Alliance / Joint Venture
- **Mechanism:** a **formal partnership** between firms — shared ownership (a JV is a separately incorporated entity) or a contractual alliance — typically pairing a foreign firm's technology/capital with a **local firm's market knowledge**.
- **Pros:** access to local knowledge, distribution, and regulatory goodwill; shared capital and risk; sometimes **required by host-country law** in restricted sectors.
- **Cons:** **shared control** (conflict over strategy), risk of **know-how leaking to the partner**, and historically high failure rates — partners often end up as competitors. (Fuller treatment of JV success/failure → [T14](T14%20-%20Joint%20Ventures.md).)

## 3. Quick comparison table

| Mode | Commitment | Control | Risk exposure | Typical use case |
|------|-----------|---------|---------------|------------------|
| Export / Import | Low | Low | Low | Testing a market; high value-to-weight goods |
| Portfolio Investment | None operational | None | Financial only | Diversification (not a way to "enter" operationally) |
| Manufacturing under License | Low | Low | Low | Simple, codifiable IP; market blocked by tariffs |
| Franchising | Low–medium | Medium (via the rulebook) | Low–medium | Services with a strong brand (fast food, hotels) |
| Turnkey Project | Medium (project-long) | None post-handover | Medium | Heavy engineering in closed markets |
| JV / Strategic Alliance | High | Shared | High | Need local knowledge or law requires a local partner |
| FDI | Highest | Full | Highest | Protect know-how; tight strategic control |

*Source logic:* Hill's Figure 8.4 decision tree — transport costs + tariffs → exporting; know-how amenable to licensing → license; tight control required or know-how not contractable → FDI. (See [T11 §3.2](T11%20-%20FDI%20vs%20Licensing.md).)

## 4. Key terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Modes of foreign market entry | Alternative methods by which a firm can serve a foreign market: Export/Import, Portfolio Investment, Manufacturing under License, Franchising, Turnkey, JV/Alliance, FDI. |
| Export / Import | Cross-border sale or purchase of finished goods and services with no foreign production. |
| Portfolio Investment | Passive cross-border investment in foreign securities without managerial control. |
| FDI (Foreign Direct Investment) | Equity investment giving the investor **control** over a foreign operation (typically ≥10% of capital); takes the form of **greenfield** or **acquisition**. |
| Greenfield investment | Building a new foreign facility from scratch. |
| Acquisition / brownfield | Entering via purchase of an existing foreign firm. |
| Turnkey project | Contractor designs, builds, trains, and hands over a ready-to-operate facility ("turn the key"). |
| Manufacturing under license | Foreign licensee produces using the licensor's technology/brand in return for royalties. |
| Licensor / Licensee | Owner of the IP granting rights (licensor) / firm receiving and using the rights (licensee). |
| Royalty | Fee paid by the licensee/franchisee for use of the licensor's technology or brand. |
| Franchising | Licensing variant in which the licensor also imposes strict operating rules and provides ongoing support around a brand. |
| Maquiladora | Mexican 1965 program allowing duty-free import of foreign inputs for assembly in border cities with mandatory re-export; US tariff applies only to Mexican value added. |
| Strategic alliance | Formal contractual collaboration between firms short of a JV (no separate entity). |
| Joint Venture (JV) | Separately incorporated entity jointly owned by two or more firms, typically foreign + local. |

## 7. Watch out

- **7 vs. 6 vs. 8.** The course-summary list used above is **7**. The teacher sometimes says **6** (collapsing Export/Import to one item and **excluding Portfolio Investment** as a purely personal/financial mode) and the Lecture 21 notes at one point draft **8** (splitting Maquiladora out from Manufacturing under License). Treat **Maquiladora as a subspecies of licensing-based manufacturing**, not a separate mode. If the exam wording says "six," drop Portfolio Investment; if it says "seven," keep it.
- **Portfolio ≠ FDI.** Both sit on the BoP capital account, but **FDI involves control (≥10%)** and operational commitment; portfolio investment is passive and financial. Don't conflate them.
- **Franchising is a *type* of licensing**, not a separate category in some textbooks — but the course treats it as its own mode because of the rulebook + brand + support bundle.
- **Turnkey transfers know-how permanently.** Unlike FDI, when the project ends there's **no long-term presence** — and the client may become a competitor. This is the key con on the exam.
- **Maquiladora dates and details:** **1965** is the program's founding year (not 1930s — Cárdenas-era closed-economy is the *context*, not the program start). The tariff specificity — **US tariff only on the Mexican value added** — is the detail the teacher wants to see.
- **"Manufacturing under license" vs. "licensing":** on the exam, treat them as synonymous; the course uses "manufacturing under license" because it's the form most commonly contrasted with FDI.
