## 1. TL;DR

Legal systems differ fundamentally across countries, and those differences directly shape how a multinational corporation writes contracts, protects intellectual property, manages product-liability exposure, and resolves disputes. The two systems central to this course are **common law** (Anglo-Saxon — judge-made, precedent-driven) and **civil law** (Roman / code law — statute-driven, codified). A third family, **theocratic law**, is relevant in some markets but is not the course's focus. For an MNC the practical consequences are concentrated in four areas: contract drafting and enforcement, property and IP rights, product-liability / tort exposure, and corruption / dispute-resolution risk.

## 2. The three main legal systems

### 2.1 Common law (Anglo-Saxon)

- Based on **tradition, precedent, and custom**; judges' decisions build the law case-by-case.
- Characterized by evolving case law; novel situations are resolved by interpreting earlier rulings.
- **Countries:** UK, USA, Canada (except Quebec), Australia, most former British colonies.
- **Implications for MNCs:**
  - More **flexibility** in applying the law to novel commercial situations.
  - **Less predictability** when no precedent exists — outcomes depend on judicial interpretation.
  - Contracts tend to be **long and detailed**, spelling out every contingency because the contract itself is the primary source of obligations.
  - Case-law research is a routine legal-compliance cost.

### 2.2 Civil law (Roman / code law)

- Based on **detailed legal codes** (codified statutes).
- Judges **apply the code**; judicial precedent carries much less formal weight than in common-law systems.
- **Countries:** Mexico, France, Germany, most of Latin America, most of continental Europe.
- **Implications for MNCs:**
  - More **predictability** — the code provides a clear statutory answer in most cases.
  - **Less flexibility** when the code does not anticipate a situation.
  - Contracts tend to be **shorter** because the civil code fills in default terms and gaps.

### 2.3 Theocratic law

- Based on **religious teachings** (most prominently **Sharia law** in some Islamic countries).
- Not the focus of this course, but worth naming for completeness — it does affect MNCs operating in certain markets (e.g. finance structures that must avoid interest, product restrictions, contract-interpretation norms).

## 3. Why legal systems matter for MNCs

An MNC crossing borders does not just encounter different taxes and different cultures — it encounters different *rules for enforcing commercial relationships*. The main exposures are:

- **Contract enforcement** — whether and how quickly a contract can be enforced differs widely across jurisdictions.
- **Property rights and intellectual property** — patent, trademark, and copyright protection vary enormously; weak enforcement is a material risk for knowledge-intensive investment.
- **Product liability** — which party bears liability for defective products, and how damages are calculated, differs across systems (see §5, Torts).
- **Corruption exposure** — operating in a high-corruption environment creates legal exposure at home under extraterritorial statutes (**US Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA)**, **UK Bribery Act**).
- **Dispute resolution** — national courts are slow, foreign, and often perceived as biased against foreign firms; MNCs increasingly use **international arbitration** to avoid those risks.

## 4. Contracts in international business

- **Common-law contracts** tend to be **long and exhaustive** — every contingency spelled out, because the written contract is the primary source of obligations.
- **Civil-law contracts** tend to be **shorter** — the civil code supplies default terms, so the contract need only specify what the parties want to *change* from the default.
- MNCs often use **hybrid templates** that combine both drafting traditions, and they explicitly specify the **governing law** (choice-of-law clause) and the **dispute-resolution forum** (choice-of-forum clause).
- **International arbitration** is commonly preferred over national courts for cross-border disputes. Well-known arbitration venues include the **ICC** (International Chamber of Commerce, Paris), the **LCIA** (London Court of International Arbitration), and the **AAA / ICDR** (American Arbitration Association / International Centre for Dispute Resolution).
- Arbitration awards are enforceable across most countries under the **New York Convention (1958)**.

## 5. Torts — *the teacher explicitly said he would define this*

- A **tort** is a **civil wrong** that causes someone loss or harm and gives rise to **legal liability** for the person who committed the wrong.
- Common categories:
  - **Negligence** — failure to exercise reasonable care.
  - **Product liability** — harm caused by a defective product.
  - **Defamation** — wrongful injury to reputation.
  - **Nuisance** — unreasonable interference with another's use of property.
- **Tort law varies dramatically** across legal systems: **US tort awards are typically much larger** than awards in civil-law countries (a point often made in Hill Ch. 2 and standard in IB courses).
- **Implications for MNCs:** product design, labelling, warranty language, recall procedures, and liability-insurance decisions all depend on the tort exposure of each market the firm sells into.

## 6. Property rights and intellectual property

- **Intellectual property (IP)** includes **patents, trademarks, and copyrights**.
- Enforcement of IP **varies widely** across legal systems, independent of what the statutes say on paper.
- Weak IP enforcement is a **key risk for knowledge-intensive FDI** — high-technology firms, pharmaceutical firms, and brand-driven firms face direct exposure when entering jurisdictions with poor enforcement.
- Multilateral regimes (e.g. **TRIPS** under the WTO, the **Paris Convention**, the **Berne Convention**) set minimum standards, but national enforcement still varies.

## 8. Key terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Common law** | Legal system based on judicial precedent and custom; judges build the law case-by-case. Anglo-Saxon tradition. |
| **Civil law** | Legal system based on detailed codified statutes; judges apply the code and precedent has limited formal weight. Roman tradition. |
| **Theocratic law** | Legal system based on religious teachings (e.g. Sharia law). |
| **Tort** | A civil wrong causing loss or harm to another, giving rise to legal liability. |
| **Negligence** | Tort based on failure to exercise reasonable care. |
| **Product liability** | Liability for harm caused by a defective product. |
| **Contract** | A legally binding agreement between parties. |
| **Choice-of-law clause** | Contract term specifying which jurisdiction's law governs the agreement. |
| **Choice-of-forum clause** | Contract term specifying where disputes will be heard (a court or arbitral institution). |
| **International arbitration** | Private dispute-resolution mechanism used in cross-border contracts; awards enforceable under the New York Convention (1958). |
| **ICC / LCIA / AAA-ICDR** | Major international arbitration institutions (Paris / London / New York). |
| **FCPA** | *Foreign Corrupt Practices Act* — US statute prohibiting bribery of foreign officials; applies extraterritorially. |
| **UK Bribery Act** | UK equivalent; broader in scope than the FCPA in some respects. |
| **Intellectual property (IP)** | Legal rights over creations of the mind — patents, trademarks, copyrights, trade secrets. |
| **TRIPS** | *Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights* — the WTO's minimum IP-standards agreement. |
