## 1. TL;DR
Tariffs are taxes on imports (duties = tariffs = taxes, per the instructor). They can be justified narrowly — protecting infant industries, generating revenue, countering unfair foreign subsidies, or pressuring markets to open — but the mainstream economic view in Hill Ch. 7 is that on balance they raise consumer prices, misallocate resources, reduce competition, and invite retaliation that escalates into welfare-destroying trade wars. A defensible exam answer takes a **balanced position**: tariffs are a legitimate but second-best tool, appropriate in specific cases and harmful as a general policy. The instructor explicitly warned against extremism (neither 0 % nor 100 %) and against turning the essay into a political piece about any single leader.

## 2. Types of tariff barriers
Tariffs are typically levied as a percentage of the invoice value (built-up inputs).

- **Ad valorem** — charged as a percentage of the declared value of the imported good (e.g., 10 % of invoice).
- **Specific** — a fixed monetary amount per physical unit (per kg, per litre, per item), regardless of value.
- **Variable** — the rate changes with market conditions, typically to keep the landed price of imports above a target floor (common in agriculture).
- **Official price lists** — the state sets an administrative reference price for customs valuation instead of using the invoice, so the importer pays duty on the official price even if the real invoice is lower (used historically by protectionist Mexico).
- **Combinations (compound)** — mix of ad valorem plus specific (e.g., 5 % of value *plus* a fixed amount per unit).

## 3. Non-tariff barriers (for context)
Tariffs are only one instrument of trade policy. Non-tariff barriers (NTBs) achieve similar protectionist effects without a formal duty:

- **Import quotas** — quantitative ceilings on imports (*Book: p. 197*).
- **Voluntary export restraints (VERs)** — exporting country "voluntarily" caps its shipments, usually under political pressure.
- **Import licenses** — administrative permission required to import (in pre-1989 Mexico these covered 100 % of imports in 1982, still 22 % by 1989).
- **Subsidies** — domestic producers receive state support, outcompeting imports on price.
- **Product standards / technical regulations** — health, safety, labelling, or environmental rules used as disguised barriers.
- **"Buy national" campaigns** — government procurement preferences and consumer nudges favouring domestic goods.
- **Currency manipulation** — undervaluing the currency to make imports expensive and exports cheap.
- **Sanctions and embargos** — political bans on trade with specific countries or sectors.
- **Export tariffs and bans** (*Book: p. 198*) — restricting outbound trade, usually to keep strategic goods at home.

## 4. Arguments *for* tariffs
- **Protection of domestic jobs and industries** — sheltering workers from cheaper foreign competition (*Book: p. 201*). Most politically salient argument.
- **Infant-industry argument** — temporary protection lets a new domestic sector reach minimum efficient scale before facing world competition.
- **Government revenue** — simple to collect at the border; historically important for developing states with weak internal tax systems.
- **Countering unfair trade practices** — retaliating against foreign dumping or subsidies that distort the playing field.
- **Leverage to open foreign markets** — tariffs (or the threat of them) as a bargaining chip in trade negotiations.
- **National security and strategic sectors** — preserving domestic capacity in defence, food, energy, or critical technology.

## 5. Arguments *against* tariffs
- **Higher consumer prices** — the tariff is passed through to buyers, lowering real income.
- **Reduced competition and efficiency** — shielded domestic firms face less pressure to innovate or cut costs.
- **Resource misallocation** — production is pulled into locations where it is not globally cost-efficient, reducing overall welfare.
- **Retaliation and trade wars** (*Book: p. 202*) — trading partners respond in kind; escalation hurts exporters on all sides and shrinks total trade.
- **Capture and rent-seeking** — protected industries lobby to keep tariffs indefinitely; "temporary" protection becomes permanent.
- **Weakens the multilateral trading system** and the predictability that global supply chains depend on.

## 6. Balanced conclusion (for the essay)
A defensible opinion for the exam:

- Tariffs have a **legitimate but narrow role**: infant-industry protection, responding to genuinely unfair foreign subsidies, and safeguarding national-security sectors.
- As a **general policy instrument** they reduce economic welfare, raise consumer prices, misallocate resources, and risk retaliation.
- The policy judgement depends on **the relative strength of domestic industries and the state's efficiency** at collecting and administering the tariff.
- Historical evidence cuts both ways: long-term heavy protectionism (e.g., Argentina, pre-1989 Mexico) produced disappointing outcomes, but opening too fast without supporting industry can also fail.
- Therefore: avoid extremism — neither 0 % free-market fundamentalism nor 100 % closed protectionism — and "find a good balance."

## 7. Key terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Tariff | Tax levied on imported (sometimes exported) goods at the border. Equated by the instructor with "duties" and "taxes on imports." |
| Ad valorem tariff | Tariff set as a percentage of the value of the imported good. |
| Specific tariff | Tariff set as a fixed amount per physical unit (e.g., €2 per kg). |
| Variable tariff | Tariff whose rate adjusts with market conditions to maintain a target landed price. |
| Official price list | Administrative reference price set by the importing state and used for customs valuation instead of the actual invoice. |
| Compound / combined tariff | Tariff that combines an ad valorem component with a specific component. |
| Non-tariff barrier (NTB) | Any trade-restricting measure that is not a formal import duty — quotas, licenses, standards, subsidies, VERs, buy-national policies, currency manipulation, sanctions. |
| Import quota | Quantitative limit on the volume or value of a good that may be imported in a period. *Book: p. 197* |
| Voluntary export restraint (VER) | Quota imposed by the exporting country, usually under political pressure from the importer. |
| Import license | Administrative permission required to import, often rationed to achieve de facto quotas. |
| Subsidy | Financial support from the state to a domestic producer, which has an equivalent protectionist effect to a tariff on the competing import. |
| Infant-industry argument | Justification for temporary protection of a new domestic industry until it reaches competitive scale. |
| Retaliation | Imposition of counter-tariffs or counter-restrictions by a trading partner, risking a trade war. *Book: p. 202* |
| Trade war | Escalating cycle of tariffs and counter-tariffs that reduces total trade and welfare on all sides. |
| Dumping | Selling exports below cost or below home-market price; a common stated justification for protective (anti-dumping) tariffs. |

Tariffs can be justified in international trade because they protect domestic industries and jobs from foreign competition, generate government revenue, and can be used strategically to counter unfair trade practices or pressure other countries to open their markets. However, overall they tend to have more negative than positive effects because they raise prices for consumers, reduce competition, and lead to inefficient allocation of resources by encouraging production in locations where it is not cost efficient. They may also trigger retaliation from other countries, resulting in trade wars that harm all parties. So while tariffs can be useful in specific situations — such as protecting infant industries or responding to unfair subsidies — in general they reduce overall economic welfare and are considered harmful to global trade efficiency.

**Optional extension for a stronger answer** (drawing on the exam-1 foundation material): note the full taxonomy of tariff barriers (ad valorem, specific, variable, official price lists, combinations) and the complementary non-tariff barriers (quotas, VERs, import licenses, subsidies, standards, buy-national campaigns, currency manipulation, sanctions). The historical experience of **protectionist Mexico** illustrates the trade-off: in 1982 import licenses covered 100 % of imports and the average tariff was 27 %, with official price lists used for customs valuation; by 1989 licenses covered only 22 % of imports and the average tariff had fallen to 10.1 %, supporting subsequent integration through NAFTA/TMEC. This shows that tariffs and NTBs are rarely used in isolation and that liberalisation is usually gradual.
